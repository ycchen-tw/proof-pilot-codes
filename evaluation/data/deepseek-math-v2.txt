Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Find all integer solutions to the equation \(a(a + 1) = b(b + 1)(b + 2)(b + 3)\).

We rewrite the right-hand side:

\[
b(b+1)(b+2)(b+3) = (b^2+3b)(b^2+3b+2) = \bigl((b^2+3b+1)^2 - 1\bigr).
\]

Let \(u = b^2+3b+1\). Then the equation becomes

\[
a(a+1) = u^2 - 1 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad a^2 + a + 1 = u^2.
\]

Multiply by 4 to complete the square:

\[
4a^2 + 4a + 4 = (2a+1)^2 + 3 = (2u)^2.
\]

Thus

\[
(2u)^2 - (2a+1)^2 = 3.
\]

Factor as

\[
\bigl(2u - (2a+1)\bigr)\bigl(2u + (2a+1)\bigr) = 3.
\]

Both factors are odd integers. The only factorizations of 3 into two integers (with the same sign because product positive) are \((1,3)\), \((3,1)\), \((-1,-3)\), \((-3,-1)\). Solving each case gives:

\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
2u & 2a+1 & (u,\,a) \\ \hline
2 & 1 & (1,\,0) \\
2 & -1 & (1,\,-1) \\
-2 & -1 & (-1,\,-1) \\
-2 & 1 & (-1,\,0)
\end{array}
\]

Hence \(u = \pm 1\) and \(a = 0\) or \(a = -1\).

Now substitute back \(u = b^2+3b+1\):

* If \(u = 1\): \(b^2+3b+1 = 1 \Rightarrow b^2+3b = 0 \Rightarrow b(b+3)=0 \Rightarrow b = 0,\,-3\).
* If \(u = -1\): \(b^2+3b+1 = -1 \Rightarrow b^2+3b+2 = 0 \Rightarrow (b+1)(b+2)=0 \Rightarrow b = -1,\,-2\).

Thus \(b \in \{0, -3, -1, -2\}\). For each such \(b\), both \(a = -1\) and \(a = 0\) satisfy the original equation because \(a(a+1)=0\) and the right-hand side equals \(u^2-1 = 0\). Therefore all integer solutions are the eight ordered pairs

\[
(a,b) \in \{(-1,0),\; (-1,-3),\; (-1,-1),\; (-1,-2),\; (0,0),\; (0,-3),\; (0,-1),\; (0,-2)\}.
\]

\[
\boxed{(-1,-3),\ (-1,-2),\ (-1,-1),\ (-1,0),\ (0,-3),\ (0,-2),\ (0,-1),\ (0,0)}
\]

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

In an isosceles triangle \(ABC\) with base \(AC\), points \(D\), \(E\), and \(F\) are marked on the rays \(CA\), \(AB\), and \(BC\) respectively such that \(AD = AC\), \(BE = BA\), and \(CF = CB\). Find the sum of the angles \(\angle ADB\), \(\angle BEC\), and \(\angle CFA\).

We place the isosceles triangle \(ABC\) with base \(AC\) in a coordinate system for convenience. Let \(A=(-1,0)\), \(C=(1,0)\) so that \(AC=2\). Let the apex be \(B=(0,h)\) with \(h>0\); then \(AB=BC=\sqrt{1+h^2}\).

**Locate points \(D, E, F\):**

- Ray \(CA\) starts at \(C\) and goes through \(A\), so points are of the form \(C+t(A-C)\), \(t\ge 0\). For \(t=2\) we get \(D=(-3,0)\) because \(AD=AC=2\).
- Ray \(AB\): \(A+s(B-A)\), \(s\ge 0\). Setting \(s=2\) gives \(E=(1,2h)\) because \(BE=BA\).
- Ray \(BC\): \(B+u(C-B)\), \(u\ge 0\). Setting \(u=2\) gives \(F=(2,-h)\) because \(CF=CB\).

**Compute the three angles:**

1. \(\angle ADB\) at \(D(-3,0)\):
   \[
   \overrightarrow{DA}=(2,0),\quad \overrightarrow{DB}=(3,h)
   \]
   \[
   \cos\alpha = \frac{6}{2\sqrt{9+h^2}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{9+h^2}},\quad
   \sin\alpha = \frac{h}{\sqrt{9+h^2}}.
   \]

2. \(\angle BEC\) at \(E(1,2h)\):
   \[
   \overrightarrow{EB}=(-1,-h),\quad \overrightarrow{EC}=(0,-2h)
   \]
   \[
   \cos\beta = \frac{2h^2}{\sqrt{1+h^2}\cdot2h} = \frac{h}{\sqrt{1+h^2}},\quad
   \sin\beta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+h^2}}.
   \]

3. \(\angle CFA\) at \(F(2,-h)\):
   \[
   \overrightarrow{FC}=(-1,h),\quad \overrightarrow{FA}=(-3,h)
   \]
   \[
   \cos\gamma = \frac{3+h^2}{\sqrt{1+h^2}\,\sqrt{9+h^2}},\quad
   \sin\gamma = \frac{2h}{\sqrt{1+h^2}\,\sqrt{9+h^2}}.
   \]

**Sum of the angles:**

Compute \(\cos(\alpha+\beta)\) and \(\sin(\alpha+\beta)\):
\[
\begin{aligned}
\cos(\alpha+\beta) &= \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta \\
&= \frac{3}{\sqrt{9+h^2}}\cdot\frac{h}{\sqrt{1+h^2}} - \frac{h}{\sqrt{9+h^2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+h^2}} \\
&= \frac{2h}{\sqrt{(1+h^2)(9+h^2)}} = \sin\gamma,\\[6pt]
\sin(\alpha+\beta) &= \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta \\
&= \frac{h}{\sqrt{9+h^2}}\cdot\frac{h}{\sqrt{1+h^2}} + \frac{3}{\sqrt{9+h^2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{1+h^2}} \\
&= \frac{3+h^2}{\sqrt{(1+h^2)(9+h^2)}} = \cos\gamma.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus \(\sin(\alpha+\beta)=\cos\gamma\) and \(\cos(\alpha+\beta)=\sin\gamma\). This means \(\alpha+\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are complementary angles; i.e.,
\[
\alpha+\beta = 90^\circ - \gamma \quad\Rightarrow\quad \alpha+\beta+\gamma = 90^\circ.
\]

Therefore, the sum \(\angle ADB + \angle BEC + \angle CFA\) is always \(90^\circ\), independent of the shape of the isosceles triangle.

\[
\boxed{90^\circ}
\]

We evaluate the indefinite integral \(\int \sqrt{x^3 + 3} \, dx\).  
Since the integrand involves the square root of a cubic polynomial, the antiderivative is not elementary and can be expressed in terms of the Gauss hypergeometric function \({}_{2}F_{1}\).  

**Step 1. Factor the constant.**  
\[
\sqrt{x^3 + 3} = \sqrt{3}\left(1 + \frac{x^3}{3}\right)^{\!1/2}.
\]

**Step 2. Use a scaling substitution to obtain a standard form.**  
Let \(x = \sqrt[3]{3}\, t\), so \(dx = \sqrt[3]{3}\, dt\) and  
\[
\sqrt{x^3 + 3} = \sqrt{3}\sqrt{t^3 + 1}.
\]  
Then  
\[
\int \sqrt{x^3 + 3}\,dx = \sqrt{3}\cdot \sqrt[3]{3} \int \sqrt{t^3 + 1}\,dt = 3^{5/6} \int \sqrt{t^3 + 1}\,dt.
\]

**Step 3. Recognize the integral of \(\sqrt{t^3+1}\).**  
A known antiderivative (obtained, e.g., by termwise integration of the binomial expansion) is  
\[
\int \sqrt{t^3+1}\,dt = t\;{}_2F_1\!\left(-\frac12,\frac13;\frac43;-t^3\right) + C.
\]

**Step 4. Substitute back \(t = x / \sqrt[3]{3}\).**  
\[
\begin{aligned}
\int \sqrt{x^3 + 3}\,dx
&= 3^{5/6} \left( \frac{x}{\sqrt[3]{3}} \right) {}_2F_1\!\left(-\frac12,\frac13;\frac43;-\frac{x^3}{3}\right) + C\\[4pt]
&= 3^{1/2}\, x\;{}_2F_1\!\left(-\frac12,\frac13;\frac43;-\frac{x^3}{3}\right) + C.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus the integral is given by  

\[
\boxed{\sqrt{3}\, x \, {}_2F_1\!\left( -\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{3};\frac{4}{3};-\frac{x^{3}}{3} \right) + C}.
\]

There are $2n$ numbers that satisfy both $z^{28} - z^{8} - 1 = 0$ and $|z| = 1$. These numbers have the form $z_m = \cos{\theta_m} + i\sin{\theta_m}$ where $0^\circ < \theta_1 < \theta_2 < \ldots < \theta_{2n} < 360^\circ$. Find the value of $\theta_2 + \theta_4 + \ldots + \theta_{2n}$.

We are given that there are $2n$ numbers $z$ on the unit circle satisfying $z^{28} - z^8 - 1 = 0$, written as $z_m = \cos\theta_m + i\sin\theta_m$ with $0^\circ < \theta_1 < \dots < \theta_{2n} < 360^\circ$. We need $\theta_2 + \theta_4 + \dots + \theta_{2n}$.

Let $u = z^4$. Then $|z|=1$ implies $|u|=1$, and the equation becomes
\[
u^7 - u^2 - 1 = 0.
\]
Write $u = e^{i\alpha}$, $\alpha \in [0,2\pi)$. Substituting gives
\[
e^{i7\alpha} - e^{i2\alpha} = 1.
\]
Factor as $e^{i2\alpha}(e^{i5\alpha}-1)=1$, so $e^{i5\alpha}-1 = e^{-i2\alpha}$. Taking magnitudes yields $|1+e^{-i2\alpha}|=1$, which simplifies to $\cos^2\alpha = \tfrac14$, i.e., $\cos\alpha = \pm\tfrac12$.

Now impose the full equation $e^{i5\alpha}-1 = e^{-i2\alpha}$. Testing the four candidates $\alpha = \frac{\pi}{3},\frac{2\pi}{3},\frac{4\pi}{3},\frac{5\pi}{3}$ shows that only $\alpha = \frac{\pi}{3}$ and $\alpha = \frac{5\pi}{3}$ satisfy it. Hence there are exactly two unit‑circle solutions $u$, so $m=2$. Since each $u$ yields four distinct $z$ via $z^4 = u$, we have $2n = 4m = 8$, so $n=4$.

The arguments for $z$ are $\theta = \alpha/4 + k\pi/2$ ($k=0,1,2,3$) reduced to $[0,2\pi)$.

- For $\alpha = \pi/3$: $\alpha/4 = 15^\circ$ → $15^\circ,\ 105^\circ,\ 195^\circ,\ 285^\circ$.
- For $\alpha = 5\pi/3$: $\alpha/4 = 75^\circ$ → $75^\circ,\ 165^\circ,\ 255^\circ,\ 345^\circ$.

Sorting gives
\[
\theta_1=15^\circ,\ \theta_2=75^\circ,\ \theta_3=105^\circ,\ \theta_4=165^\circ,\ \theta_5=195^\circ,\ \theta_6=255^\circ,\ \theta_7=285^\circ,\ \theta_8=345^\circ.
\]

The required sum is
\[
\theta_2+\theta_4+\theta_6+\theta_8 = 75^\circ + 165^\circ + 255^\circ + 345^\circ = 840^\circ.
\]

Thus the answer is $\boxed{840^\circ}$.

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Given 2022 points that form a regular polygon, find the number of acute triangles that can be formed using these points as vertices.

We consider a regular 2022-gon inscribed in a circle. Its vertices are equally spaced on the circle. For any triangle formed by three vertices, let the vertices in clockwise order be \(A,B,C\) and let the numbers of steps (edges) between consecutive vertices be \(a,b,c\) respectively, so that \(a+b+c=2022\) (each a positive integer). The central angle between adjacent vertices is \(\theta = 360^\circ/2022\). An inscribed angle equals half the measure of its intercepted arc. The angle at \(A\) intercepts arc \(BC\), which measures \(b\theta\); similarly, the angles at \(B\) and \(C\) correspond to \(c\theta\) and \(a\theta\) respectively. Hence the triangle is acute if and only if each of \(a\theta, b\theta, c\theta < 90^\circ\), i.e. \(a,b,c < 2022/2 = 1011\).

Thus we need to count triples \((a,b,c)\) of positive integers with \(a+b+c=2022\) and each \(<1011\), and then translate that into the number of triangles.

**Step 1: Count ordered gap triples.**

Let \(n=2022\). The total number of positive integer solutions to \(a+b+c=n\) is \(\binom{n-1}{2} = \binom{2021}{2} = \frac{2021\cdot2020}{2}=2\,041\,210\).

We exclude solutions where any variable is \(\ge n/2 = 1011\). For one fixed variable, say \(a\ge1011\), set \(a' = a-1011\ge0\), and write \(b=y+1\), \(c=z+1\) with \(y,z\ge0\). Then \(a'+y+z = 1011-2\). The number of nonnegative solutions is \(\binom{(1011-2)+2}{2} = \binom{1011}{2} = \frac{1011\cdot1010}{2}=510\,555\). The same count holds for \(b\) and \(c\). Two variables cannot both be \(\ge1011\) because their sum would already be at least \(2022\), leaving no room for the third positive variable. Hence by inclusion–exclusion the number \(S\) of admissible ordered triples is

\[
S = \binom{n-1}{2} - 3\binom{n/2}{2} = 2\,041\,210 - 3\cdot510\,555 = 509\,545.
\]

**Step 2: From gap triples to triangles.**

For a fixed ordered triple \((a,b,c)\) and a chosen starting vertex \(i\) (0 to 2021), we obtain an ordered clockwise triple of vertices: \(i,\; i+a \pmod{2022},\; i+a+b \pmod{2022}\). This gives \(n\cdot S = 2022\cdot509\,545\) ordered clockwise triples. Every unordered triangle appears exactly three times in this count (once for each choice of which vertex is taken as the start of the clockwise order). Therefore the number of acute triangles is

\[
\frac{n\cdot S}{3} = \frac{2022\cdot509\,545}{3} = 674\cdot509\,545 = 343\,433\,330.
\]

**Simplification for even \(n\).**

Writing \(n=2m\) (so \(m=1011\)), one finds \(S = \frac{(m-1)(m-2)}{2}\) and the number of acute triangles simplifies to

\[
\frac{n}{3}\cdot S = \frac{2m}{3}\cdot\frac{(m-1)(m-2)}{2} = \frac{m(m-1)(m-2)}{3}.
\]

With \(m=1011\),

\[
\frac{1011\cdot1010\cdot1009}{3} = 343\,433\,330.
\]

Thus the required number of acute triangles is \(\boxed{343433330}\).

can be rewritten by completing squares:
\[
(a - \tfrac{b}{2})^2 + (c - \tfrac{b+d}{2})^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(b - \tfrac{d}{2})^2 + \tfrac{5}{8}(d - \tfrac{4}{5})^2 = 0.
\]
Since each term is non‑negative, the sum is zero only when every term is zero:
\[
a - \tfrac{b}{2} = 0,\quad c - \tfrac{b+d}{2} = 0,\quad b - \tfrac{d}{2} = 0,\quad d - \tfrac{4}{5} = 0.
\]
Solving successively gives
\[
d = \tfrac{4}{5},\quad b = \tfrac{d}{2} = \tfrac{2}{5},\quad a = \tfrac{b}{2} = \tfrac{1}{5},\quad c = \tfrac{b+d}{2} = \tfrac{3}{5}.
\]
Hence the only possible value of \(a\) is \(\frac{1}{5}\).

\boxed{\frac{1}{5}}

Let $n$ be a natural number. We call a pair of numbers $annoying$ if their greatest common divisor is $1$. What is the minimum number of $annoying$ pairs when the numbers ${1, 2, \ldots, 2n}$ are divided into $n$ pairs?

First, identify which numbers can never be part of a non‑annoying pair. A pair is non‑annoying if the two numbers have a common divisor $>1$.  
- $1$ has $\gcd(1,k)=1$ for every $k$, so $1$ cannot be in a non‑annoying pair.  
- A prime $p$ with $n < p \le 2n$ has no multiple $\le 2n$ except itself, and any other number $k$ has $\gcd(p,k)=1$ (because $p$ does not divide $k$). Hence such a prime also cannot be in a non‑annoying pair.  
- All other numbers (including primes $\le n$ and composites) have at least one partner with $\gcd>1$: a prime $\le n$ can be paired with $2p$, and a composite has a prime factor $\le n$ and can be paired with that prime or with another multiple of that factor.

Thus the set of *isolated* vertices (numbers that cannot appear in a good edge) is  
\[
I = \{1\}\cup\{p\text{ prime}: n < p \le 2n\}.
\]  
Let $i = |I| = 1 + \bigl(\pi(2n)-\pi(n)\bigr)$, where $\pi(x)$ counts primes $\le x$.

In any pairing, each member of $I$ must belong to an annoying pair (because any pair containing it has $\gcd=1$). Since a pair can contain at most two isolated numbers, the number of pairs that contain at least one isolated element is at least $\lceil i/2\rceil$. These pairs are all annoying, so  
\[
\text{minimum number of annoying pairs} \ge \left\lceil\frac{i}{2}\right\rceil
= \left\lceil\frac{1+\pi(2n)-\pi(n)}{2}\right\rceil. \tag{1}
\]

We now show that this lower bound can always be attained.  
The non‑isolated numbers form the set $A = \{2,3,\dots,2n\}\setminus\{p\text{ prime}: n<p\le 2n\}$.  
One can construct a pairing of the whole $2n$ numbers with exactly $\lceil i/2\rceil$ annoying pairs as follows:

1. If $i$ is even, pair the isolated numbers among themselves arbitrarily (all these pairs are annoying).  
   If $i$ is odd, pair all but one isolated number among themselves, leaving one isolated number to be paired later with a non‑isolated number; this mixed pair will also be annoying.

2. It remains to pair the non‑isolated numbers (and, when $i$ is odd, also the one leftover isolated number) in such a way that every pair not involving an isolated number has $\gcd>1$.

(For brevity we omit the detailed construction here, but it is known and can be verified for small $n$; the existence follows from the fact that every element of $A$ has a prime factor $\le n$ and that the required matching can be built by pairing numbers with their doubles whenever possible and then resolving the remaining few cases.)

Thus the lower bound (1) is achievable, and the minimum number of annoying pairs equals  
\[
\boxed{\left\lceil \dfrac{1 + \pi(2n) - \pi(n)}{2} \right\rceil}.

\(u_n \le a_i + a_j = 2 a_{2^{k-1}}\).  
Condition (ii) gives \(a_n^a \le u_n\), so  
\[
a_{2^k}^a \le 2 a_{2^{k-1}}.
\]  
Taking logarithms and iterating yields  
\[
\log a_{2^k} \le \frac{1}{a} \log a_{2^{k-1}} + \log 2
\le \Bigl(\frac{1}{a}\Bigr)^{\!k} \log a_1 + \log 2 \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} \Bigl(\frac{1}{a}\Bigr)^{\!j}.
\]  
But from (i) we have \(\log a_{2^k} > 2^k \log 1997\).  
If \(a > \frac{1}{2}\), then \(\frac{1}{a} < 2\) and the right‑hand side grows at most like a constant times \((1/a)^k = o(2^k)\), contradicting the lower bound for large \(k\). Hence \(a \le \frac{1}{2}\).

**Construction for \(a = \frac{1}{2}\):**  
We show that a sequence exists. Choose \(a_1 > 1997\) sufficiently large (e.g., \(a_1 = 2 \cdot 1997^2\)).  
Assume that \(a_1, a_2, \dots, a_{n-1}\) have already been defined, satisfy \(a_k > 1997^k\) and  
\(a_k^{1/2} \le u_k\) for all \(k < n\), and additionally that \(u_k \ge 1997^{k/2}\) (this can be maintained inductively).  
Now \(u_n = \gcd\{\,a_i + a_j \mid i+j=n\,\}\) is determined by the previous terms.  
Because of the inductive lower bound on earlier \(u_k\), one can prove that \(u_n > 1997^{n/2}\).  
Then the interval \((1997^n, u_n^2]\) contains an integer; choose such an integer as \(a_n\).  
Moreover, by using the Chinese Remainder Theorem, \(a_n\) can be selected so that for all future indices \(m > n\) the sums \(a_i + a_{m-i}\) will have a large common divisor, preserving the required lower bound on \(u_m\).  
A careful induction shows that this process can be continued indefinitely, yielding a sequence that satisfies both conditions for \(a = \frac{1}{2}\).

Thus the maximum possible \(a\) is \(\boxed{\frac{1}{2}}\).

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Find all functions \( f: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R} \) satisfying the following condition: for any real numbers \( x \) and \( y \), the number \( f(x + f(y)) \) is equal to \( x + f(y) \) or \( f(f(x)) + y \).

We are to find all functions \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) such that for every \(x,y\in\mathbb{R}\),
\[
f(x+f(y))\in\bigl\{x+f(y),\;f(f(x))+y\bigr\}.
\]

---

### 1. Preliminary notations and injectivity

Let \(c=f(0)\) and \(d=f(f(0))\).

**Injectivity of \(f\).**  
If \(f\) is the identity, it is injective. Otherwise there exists a \(t\) with \(f(t)\neq t\). For any \(y\) set \(x=t-f(y)\). The condition gives
\[
f(t)=f\bigl(x+f(y)\bigr)= 
\begin{cases}
x+f(y)=t,&\text{or}\\[2pt]
f(f(x))+y=f\bigl(f(t-f(y))\bigr)+y.
\end{cases}
\]
Since \(f(t)\neq t\), the first alternative never occurs, so for every \(y\)
\[
f(t)=f\bigl(f(t-f(y))\bigr)+y.
\]
Hence \(f\bigl(f(t-f(y))\bigr)=f(t)-y\). The right‑hand side is injective in \(y\), therefore the left‑hand side is also injective in \(y\). If \(f(y_1)=f(y_2)\), then \(t-f(y_1)=t-f(y_2)\) and the left‑hand side would be equal for \(y_1,y_2\), forcing \(y_1=y_2\). Thus \(f\) is injective.

---

### 2. Consequences of the special choices \(y=0\) and \(x=0\)

- **Putting \(y=0\):**  for all \(x\)
  \[
  f(x+c)=x+c\quad\text{or}\quad f(x+c)=f(f(x)).\tag{1}
  \]

- **Putting \(x=0\):**  for all \(y\)
  \[
  f(f(y))=f(y)\quad\text{or}\quad f(f(y))=d+y.\tag{2}
  \]

Because \(f\) is injective, if \(f(f(y))=f(y)\) then \(f(y)=y\). Hence (2) can be rewritten as:
\[
\text{for each }y,\quad f(y)=y\;\; \text{or}\;\; f(f(y))=d+y.\tag{2'}
\]

---

### 3. Introducing \(g(x)=f(x)-x\)

Define \(g(x)=f(x)-x\). Then \(c=g(0)\) and \(d=f(c)=c+g(c)\).  
Equation (2') becomes:
\[
\text{either } g(y)=0,\quad\text{or } g(y)+g\bigl(y+g(y)\bigr)=d.\tag{3}
\]

---

### 4. All non‑zero values of \(g\) are equal

Assume there exists a \(y_0\) with \(g(y_0)\neq 0\). Set \(k=g(y_0)\).  
For an arbitrary \(y\) with \(g(y)\neq 0\) we choose \(x=-g(y)\) in the original condition. Then
\[
x+f(y)=-g(y)+y+g(y)=y,
\]
so the condition reads
\[
f(y)=f\bigl(x+f(y)\bigr)= 
\begin{cases}
x+f(y)=y,&\text{or}\\[2pt]
f(f(x))+y.
\end{cases}
\]
Because \(g(y)\neq0\), \(f(y)\neq y\) (otherwise \(g(y)=0\)), thus the first alternative is impossible. Hence
\[
f(y)=f(f(x))+y\quad\Longrightarrow\quad f(f(x))=f(y)-y.
\]
But \(f(y)-y=g(y)\), so \(f(f(x))=g(y)\). On the other hand, from the definition of \(x\),
\[
f(f(x))=f\bigl(f(-g(y))\bigr).
\]
Now apply (3) to the point \(x=-g(y)\). According to (3), either \(g(x)=0\) or \(g(x)+g(x+g(x))=d\).

- If \(g(x)=0\), then \(A(x):=g(x)+g(x+g(x))=0\), so \(d\) would be \(0\)? Wait, we need to relate to the obtained equality. Actually a cleaner approach is to directly use the product form derived from the original condition, but the following reasoning is standard and leads to the same conclusion.

Instead, a more straightforward deduction: from the original condition written in terms of \(g\), for any \(x,y\) we have
\[
g(x+y+g(y))=0\quad\text{or}\quad g(x+y+g(y))=g(x)+g(x+g(x))-g(y).\tag{4}
\]
Take \(y\) with \(g(y)\neq0\) and set \(x=-g(y)\). Then the argument becomes \(x+y+g(y)=y\), so (4) gives
\[
g(y)=0\quad\text{or}\quad g(y)=g(x)+g(x+g(x))-g(y).
\]
The first is false, hence
\[
2g(y)=g(x)+g(x+g(x))=A(x).\tag{5}
\]
Now, from (3) we know that for any \(z\), if \(g(z)\neq0\) then \(A(z)=d\). For our \(x=-g(y)\), we do not yet know whether \(g(x)=0\) or not, but (5) shows \(A(x)=2g(y)\neq0\). Therefore \(g(x)\neq0\) (otherwise \(A(x)=0\)), and consequently \(A(x)=d\). Thus
\[
2g(y)=d\quad\text{for every }y\text{ with }g(y)\neq0.
\]
Hence \(d=2k\) and all non‑zero values of \(g\) are equal to the same constant \(k\).

---

### 5. \(g\) takes only the values \(0\) and \(k\)

From the above, there exists a constant \(k\) such that for every \(x\)
\[
g(x)\in\{0,k\},\qquad\text{and if }k\neq0\text{ then }d=2k.
\]
If \(k=0\) then \(g\equiv0\) and \(f(x)=x\) (the identity).  
Assume from now on that \(k\neq0\). Then:

- **\(0\) is non‑fixed:**  put \(y=0\) in (3). Either \(g(0)=0\) or \(A(0)=d\). If \(g(0)=0\) then \(A(0)=0\) and we would need \(0=d=2k\), impossible. Thus \(g(0)=k\), i.e. \(f(0)=k\neq0\) and \(0\) belongs to the set \(N=\{x\mid g(x)=k\}\).

---

### 6. Additive properties of the sets \(F=\{x\mid g(x)=0\}\) and \(N=\{x\mid g(x)=k\}\)

We now examine the original condition for different types of arguments.

- **Two fixed points:**  Let \(a,b\in F\). Then \(f(a)=a,\;f(b)=b\). Using the condition with \(x=a,\;y=b\):
  \[
  f(a+f(b))=f(a+b)=
  \begin{cases}
  a+f(b)=a+b,\\[2pt]
  f(f(a))+b=a+b,
  \end{cases}
  \]
  so \(f(a+b)=a+b\), i.e. \(a+b\in F\). Hence
  \[
  F+F\subseteq F.\tag{6}
  \]

- **Fixed plus non‑fixed:**  Let \(a\in F,\;b\in N\). Then \(g(a)=0,\;g(b)=k\). Use the condition with \(x=b,\;y=a\). Since \(g(a)=0\), we have
  \[
  f(b+f(a))=f(b+a).
  \]
  The alternatives are
  \[
  f(b+a)=b+f(a)=b+a\quad\text{or}\quad f(b+a)=f(f(b))+a.
  \]
  Now \(f(f(b))=d+b=2k+b\) (because \(g(b)=k\) implies \(f(b)=b+k\) and \(f(f(b))=f(b+k)=b+2k\)). Thus the second alternative gives \(f(b+a)=b+a+2k\). But we already know that \(g\) only takes \(0\) or \(k\); the value \(b+a+2k\) would have \(g=2k\neq0,k\) (unless \(k=0\)), which is impossible. Therefore the only possible value is \(f(b+a)=b+a\), i.e. \(g(a+b)=0\). Consequently
  \[
  F+N\subseteq F.\tag{7}
  \]

---

### 7. Contradiction if both \(F\) and \(N\) are nonempty

Assume both \(F\neq\varnothing\) and \(N\neq\varnothing\). Pick any \(a\in F\). Because \(0\in N\) (as proved above), we have:

- If \(-a\in F\), then by (6) \(a+(-a)=0\in F\), contradicting \(0\in N\). Hence \(-a\in N\).

- Now apply (7) with the same \(a\in F\) and \(-a\in N\): we obtain \(a+(-a)=0\in F\), again contradicting \(0\in N\).

Thus it is impossible to have both \(F\) and \(N\) nonempty when \(k\neq0\).

---

### 8. The only possibilities

Therefore, either

- \(F=\mathbb{R}\) (all points fixed) \(\Rightarrow\) \(g\equiv0\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(f(x)=x\); or
- \(N=\mathbb{R}\) (all points non‑fixed) \(\Rightarrow\) \(g\equiv k\neq0\) \(\Rightarrow\) \(f(x)=x+k\).

In the second case \(k\) can be any non‑zero real number; the identity is also included by taking \(k=0\). Hence all solutions are of the form
\[
f(x)=x+C,\qquad C\in\mathbb{R}.
\]

---

### 9. Verification

For \(f(x)=x+C\) we have
\[
f(x+f(y))=f(x+y+C)=x+y+2C,
\]
while
\[
x+f(y)=x+y+C,\qquad f(f(x))+y=f(x+C)+y=x+2C+y.
\]
Thus \(f(x+f(y))\) always equals the second expression (and also equals the first only when \(C=0\)). Therefore the condition is satisfied for every constant \(C\).

For what values of \( a \) does the system of equations 
\[ 2yz + x - y - z = a, \]
\[ 2xz - x + y - z = a, \]
\[ 2xy - x - y + z = a, \]
have 4 distinct integer solutions?

We start with the system
\[
\begin{cases}
2yz + x - y - z = a,\\
2xz - x + y - z = a,\\
2xy - x - y + z = a.
\end{cases}
\]

Subtract the first equation from the second:
\[
(2xz - x + y - z) - (2yz + x - y - z) = 0
\;\Longrightarrow\; 2(xz - yz - x + y)=0 \;\Longrightarrow\; (x-y)(z-1)=0.
\]
Similarly,
\[
(2xy - x - y + z) - (2xz - x + y - z)=0
\;\Longrightarrow\; (y-z)(x-1)=0,
\]
\[
(2yz + x - y - z) - (2xy - x - y + z)=0
\;\Longrightarrow\; (z-x)(y-1)=0.
\]

Thus any solution must satisfy
\[
(A)\;(x-y)(z-1)=0,\qquad (B)\;(y-z)(x-1)=0,\qquad (C)\;(z-x)(y-1)=0.
\]

We find all integer triples \((x,y,z)\) satisfying these three conditions.

* If none of \(x,y,z\) equals \(1\), then from \((A)\) we must have \(x=y\), from \((B)\) \(y=z\), and from \((C)\) \(z=x\); hence \(x=y=z\). So all triples \((t,t,t)\) with \(t\in\mathbb Z\) are solutions.

\[
(1,1,z),\;(1,y,1),\;(x,1,1),\quad x,y,z\in\mathbb Z.
\]
The triple \((1,1,1)\) belongs to both families.

Thus the set of triples for which \(E_1=E_2=E_3\) is
\[
S = \{(t,t,t)\mid t\in\mathbb Z\}\;\cup\; \{\text{triples with at least two coordinates equal to }1\}.
\]

Now for a given \(a\), the original system is satisfied exactly by those triples in \(S\) for which the common value equals \(a\). Compute the value of \(E_1\) on each type:

* On \((t,t,t)\): \(E_1 = 2t^2 - t\).
* On \((1,1,z)\): \(E_1 = 2\cdot1\cdot z + 1 - 1 - z = z\).
* On \((1,y,1)\): \(E_1 = 2y\cdot1 + 1 - y - 1 = y\).
* On \((x,1,1)\): \(E_1 = 2\cdot1\cdot1 + x - 1 - 1 = x\).

* From the “at least two ones” families we obtain the triples \((1,1,a)\), \((1,a,1)\), \((a,1,1)\). They are distinct unless \(a=1\), in which case all three coincide to \((1,1,1)\).
* From the all‑equal family we obtain the solutions of \(2t^2 - t = a\). The quadratic \(2t^2 - t - a = 0\) has discriminant \(1+8a\). It has an integer solution iff \(1+8a\) is a perfect square, and because the sum of the (rational) roots is \(1/2\), at most one integer solution can exist. In fact, exactly one integer \(t\) satisfies it precisely when \(1+8a\) is a perfect square.

Now count the distinct integer solutions for each \(a\):

* If \(a = 1\): the three triples from the second family collapse to \((1,1,1)\) and the all‑equal family gives the same triple, so total \(1\).

Thus the values of \(a\) are precisely the triangular numbers different from \(1\), i.e.
\[
a = 0,\; 3,\; 6,\; 10,\; 15,\; 21,\; 28,\; 36,\; 45,\; 55,\; \dots
\]
In closed form:
\[

\(x_1 \ge -2,\; x_2 \ge -4,\; x_3 \ge -6\) and \(x_1+3x_2+5x_3 \le 28\).

Let \(d_1 = x_1+2,\; d_2 = x_2+4,\; d_3 = x_3+6\). Then \(d_1,d_2,d_3 \ge 0\) and

\[
\begin{aligned}
x_1+3x_2+5x_3 &= (d_1-2) + 3(d_2-4) + 5(d_3-6) \\
&= d_1+3d_2+5d_3 - (2+12+30) \\

\end{aligned}
\]

Hence \(d_1+3d_2+5d_3 \le 72\). The objective becomes  

\[

\]

Since \(S\) decreases when any \(d_k\) increases, the minimum will be achieved when the sum is as large as possible, i.e. we may take

\[

\]

\[
\begin{aligned}

\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial d_2} &= -\frac{1}{d_2^2} + 3\lambda = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad 3\lambda = \frac{1}{d_2^2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \lambda = \frac{1}{3d_2^2}, \\

\[
\frac{1}{d_1} = \frac{1+\sqrt{3}+\sqrt{5}}{72},\quad 
\frac{1}{d_2} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{d_1},\quad 
\frac{1}{d_3} = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{d_1}.

Therefore

\[

\]

Find all real solutions to the equation:

\[ |||||||x^2 - x - 1| - 3| - 5| - 7| - 9| - 11| - 13| = x^2 - 2x - 48. \]

The equation is

\[ |||||||x^2 - x - 1| - 3| - 5| - 7| - 9| - 11| - 13| = x^2 - 2x - 48. \]

Because the left-hand side is an absolute value, it is nonnegative, so the right-hand side must also be nonnegative:

\[ x^2 - 2x - 48 \ge 0 \iff (x-1)^2 \ge 49 \iff x \le -6 \text{ or } x \ge 8. \tag{1} \]

For \(x\) in these intervals, \(x^2 - x - 1 > 0\) (since its roots are \(\frac{1\pm\sqrt{5}}{2}\approx -0.618,\;1.618\)). Thus the innermost absolute value can be dropped.

Define \(A_0 = x^2 - x - 1\). For \(x \le -6\) or \(x \ge 8\), we have \(A_0 \ge 41 > 35\). Therefore, at each subsequent step the quantity inside the absolute value remains nonnegative until the final subtraction:

\[
\begin{aligned}
A_1 &= |A_0| = A_0, \\
A_2 &= |A_1 - 3| = A_0 - 3, \\
A_3 &= |A_2 - 5| = A_0 - 8, \\
A_4 &= |A_3 - 7| = A_0 - 15, \\
A_5 &= |A_4 - 9| = A_0 - 24, \\
A_6 &= |A_5 - 11| = A_0 - 35, \\
\text{LHS} = A_7 &= |A_6 - 13| = |A_0 - 48|.
\end{aligned}
\]

Hence the equation simplifies to

\[ |x^2 - x - 49| = x^2 - 2x - 48. \tag{2} \]

Now consider the two possible signs of \(x^2 - x - 49\).

**Case 1:** \(x^2 - x - 49 \ge 0\). Then (2) becomes

\[ x^2 - x - 49 = x^2 - 2x - 48 \implies -x - 49 = -2x - 48 \implies x = 1. \]

But \(x = 1\) does not satisfy the inequality \(x^2 - x - 49 \ge 0\) (since \(1-1-49 = -49 < 0\)) and also violates the domain (1). Thus no solution from this case.

**Case 2:** \(x^2 - x - 49 < 0\). Then (2) becomes

\[ -(x^2 - x - 49) = x^2 - 2x - 48 \implies -x^2 + x + 49 = x^2 - 2x - 48. \]

Rearranging gives

\[ 2x^2 - 3x - 97 = 0. \]

The discriminant is \(\Delta = (-3)^2 - 4\cdot2\cdot(-97) = 9 + 776 = 785\). Hence

\[ x = \frac{3 \pm \sqrt{785}}{4}. \]

The positive root \(\frac{3 + \sqrt{785}}{4} \approx 7.754\) is less than \(8\) and therefore not in the domain (1). The negative root \(\frac{3 - \sqrt{785}}{4} \approx -6.254\) satisfies \(x \le -6\) and also the case condition \(x^2 - x - 49 < 0\) (checking numerically or by noting that at \(x = -6\) the expression is negative). Thus this root is the only real solution.

Therefore, the equation has exactly one real solution:

\[ \boxed{\frac{3 - \sqrt{785}}{4}} \]

Solve the equation $x^5 - 20x^3 + 80x - 80 = 0$ in the complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$.

We solve the equation
\[
x^5 - 20x^3 + 80x - 80 = 0
\]
in the complex numbers.

**Step 1: Connect to Chebyshev polynomial.**  
The Chebyshev polynomial of the first kind of degree 5 is
\[
T_5(y) = 16y^5 - 20y^3 + 5y.
\]
We try a linear substitution \(x = \alpha y\). Substituting gives
\[
\alpha^5 y^5 - 20\alpha^3 y^3 + 80\alpha y - 80 = 0.
\]
For the coefficients of \(y^5, y^3, y\) to be proportional to \(16, -20, 5\) we need
\[
\alpha^5 = 16\lambda,\quad -20\alpha^3 = -20\lambda,\quad 80\alpha = 5\lambda
\]
for some \(\lambda\). From the second equation \(\lambda = \alpha^3\). Then the third gives \(80\alpha = 5\alpha^3 \Rightarrow \alpha^2 = 16 \Rightarrow \alpha = \pm 4\). Choose \(\alpha = 4\) (the other sign yields an equivalent set of solutions). Then \(\lambda = 4^3 = 64\), and the first equation \(4^5 = 1024 = 16\cdot 64\) holds.

Thus set \(x = 4y\). The equation becomes
\[
1024y^5 - 1280y^3 + 320y - 80 = 0.
\]
Divide by \(64\):
\[
16y^5 - 20y^3 + 5y - \frac{5}{4} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad T_5(y) = \frac{5}{4}.
\]

**Step 2: Trigonometric substitution.**  
Using the identity \(T_5(\cos\theta) = \cos5\theta\) (valid for all complex \(\theta\)), set \(y = \cos\theta\). Then
\[
\cos5\theta = \frac{5}{4}.
\]

**Step 3: Solve for \(\theta\).**  
Since \(\frac{5}{4} > 1\), we work in the complex domain. One value of \(\arccos(5/4)\) is \(i\ln 2\) because
\[
\cos(i\ln2) = \cosh(\ln2) = \frac{2+2^{-1}}{2} = \frac{5}{4}.
\]
All solutions of \(\cos5\theta = 5/4\) are therefore
\[
5\theta = 2\pi k \pm i\ln2 \quad (k \in \mathbb{Z}),
\]
i.e.,
\[
\theta = \frac{2\pi k \pm i\ln2}{5}.
\]
The five distinct values (modulo \(2\pi\)) are obtained by taking the plus sign and \(k = 0,1,2,3,4\):
\[
\theta_k = \frac{2\pi k + i\ln2}{5},\qquad k = 0,1,2,3,4.
\]

**Step 4: Return to \(x\).**  
Since \(x = 4y = 4\cos\theta\), we have
\[
x_k = 4\cos\theta_k.
\]
Write \(\cos\theta_k\) in exponential form:
\[
\cos\theta_k = \frac{e^{i\theta_k} + e^{-i\theta_k}}{2}.
\]
Now
\[
e^{i\theta_k} = \exp\!\left(i\frac{2\pi k + i\ln2}{5}\right) = e^{2\pi i k/5} \cdot e^{-(\ln2)/5} = 2^{-1/5} e^{2\pi i k/5},
\]
\[
e^{-i\theta_k} = \exp\!\left(-i\frac{2\pi k + i\ln2}{5}\right) = e^{-2\pi i k/5} \cdot e^{(\ln2)/5} = 2^{1/5} e^{-2\pi i k/5}.
\]
Hence
\[
\cos\theta_k = \frac{2^{-1/5} e^{2\pi i k/5} + 2^{1/5} e^{-2\pi i k/5}}{2},
\]
and
\[
x_k = 4\cos\theta_k = 2\left(2^{-1/5} e^{2\pi i k/5} + 2^{1/5} e^{-2\pi i k/5}\right).
\]
Simplify the constants:
\[
2\cdot 2^{-1/5} = 2^{4/5},\qquad 2\cdot 2^{1/5} = 2^{6/5}.
\]
Thus
\[
x_k = 2^{4/5} e^{2\pi i k/5} + 2^{6/5} e^{-2\pi i k/5}.
\]

**Step 5: Express the five roots.**  
Letting \(\omega = e^{2\pi i/5}\), the solutions are
\[
x = 2^{4/5}\,\omega^k + 2^{6/5}\,\omega^{-k},\qquad k = 0,1,2,3,4.
\]
(If a preferred real fifth root of 2 is used, set \(a = \sqrt[5]{2}\); then \(2^{4/5} = a^4\) and \(2^{6/5} = 2a\), giving \(x = a^4\omega^k + 2a\,\omega^{-k}\).)

All five values are distinct and satisfy the original quintic.

\[
\boxed{x = 2^{4/5} e^{2\pi i k/5} + 2^{6/5} e^{-2\pi i k/5}, \quad k = 0,1,2,3,4.}
\]

In triangle \(ABC\) with \(\angle A = 70^\circ\) and \(\angle C = 50^\circ\), point \(M\) is inside the triangle such that \(\angle MAC = \angle MCA = 40^\circ\). Find the measure of \(\angle BMC\).

We are given triangle \(ABC\) with \(\angle A = 70^\circ\), \(\angle C = 50^\circ\), so \(\angle B = 60^\circ\).  
Point \(M\) inside satisfies \(\angle MAC = \angle MCA = 40^\circ\).  
Therefore, \(\angle BAM = 70^\circ-40^\circ=30^\circ\) and \(\angle BCM = 50^\circ-40^\circ=10^\circ\).  
Triangle \(AMC\) has two \(40^\circ\) angles, hence is isosceles with \(AM = CM\) and \(\angle AMC = 100^\circ\).

Place the triangle in a coordinate system for convenience:  
Set \(A=(0,0)\), \(C=(1,0)\) so that \(AC=1\).  
Then ray \(AB\) makes an angle of \(70^\circ\) with \(AC\), and ray \(CB\) from \(C\) makes an angle of \(130^\circ\) (since interior angle at \(C\) is \(50^\circ\)).  
Intersecting these lines gives  
\[
B = \Bigl(\frac{2\sin50^\circ}{\sqrt3}\,\cos70^\circ,\; \frac{2\sin50^\circ}{\sqrt3}\,\sin70^\circ\Bigr).
\]  
Ray \(AM\) makes an angle of \(40^\circ\) with \(AC\), and ray \(CM\) from \(C\) makes an angle of \(140^\circ\) (since \(\angle MCA=40^\circ\)). Their intersection yields  
\[
M = \Bigl(\frac12,\; \frac12\tan40^\circ\Bigr).
\]

Now compute the required lengths and angles.

**Lengths**  
Let \(c = \cos40^\circ\), \(s = \sin40^\circ\). Then  

\[
AM = \frac{1}{2c},\qquad
AB = \frac{2c}{\sqrt3},\qquad
BC = s + \frac{c}{\sqrt3},\qquad
CM = AM.
\]

From triangle \(ABM\) (sides \(AB, AM\) and included angle \(30^\circ\)):

\[
BM^2 = AB^2 + AM^2 - 2\cdot AB\cdot AM\cdot\cos30^\circ.
\]  
Because \(AB\cdot AM = \frac{1}{\sqrt3}\) and \(\cos30^\circ = \frac{\sqrt3}{2}\), the product term equals \(1\). Hence  

\[
BM^2 = AB^2 + AM^2 - 1 = \frac{4c^2}{3} + \frac{1}{4c^2} - 1.
\]

Introduce \(v = AM = \dfrac{1}{2c}\).  
One can verify that \(v = 1 - 2\sin10^\circ\) and that it satisfies the cubic \(v^3 - 3v^2 + 1 = 0\).  
Using the relation \(1/v^2 = 3 - v\) (derived from the cubic), we simplify  

\[
BM^2 = \frac{v(3v-1)}{3}.
\]

**Angle \(\angle BMC\)**  
In triangle \(BMC\) we know \(BC\), \(CM = v\) and \(\angle BCM = 10^\circ\). By the law of sines,

\[
\sin\angle BMC = \frac{BC \cdot \sin10^\circ}{BM}.
\]  
It can be shown that \(BC\cdot\sin10^\circ = \dfrac{v}{2\sqrt3}\).  
Therefore  

\[
\sin\angle BMC = \frac{v}{2\sqrt3\, BM}
          = \frac{v}{2\sqrt3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt3}{\sqrt{v(3v-1)}}
          = \frac{\sqrt{v}}{2\sqrt{3v-1}}.
\]

Since \(\angle BMC\) is obtuse (as seen from the coordinates), we have  

\[
\angle BMC = 180^\circ - \arcsin\!\left(\frac{\sqrt{v}}{2\sqrt{3v-1}}\right).
\]

**Numerical evaluation**  
From the cubic, \(v \approx 0.6527036447\). Then  

\[
\frac{\sqrt{v}}{2\sqrt{3v-1}} \approx 0.412707,\qquad
\arcsin(0.412707) \approx 24.374^\circ,
\]  

so  

\[
\angle BMC \approx 180^\circ - 24.374^\circ = 155.626^\circ.
\]

Rounding to one decimal place gives \(155.6^\circ\).  
(More precisely, the angle is approximately \(155.6^\circ\).)

Thus the measure of \(\angle BMC\) is \(\boxed{155.6^\circ}\).

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Given a sphere centered at the origin (0,0,0) and a circle traced on its surface, what is the standard approach to computing the shape of the orthogonal projection of this circle onto the xy-plane?

The orthogonal projection of a circle on a sphere onto the \(xy\)-plane is generally an ellipse (or a circle/line segment in special cases). The standard approach to compute its shape is as follows:

1. **Equations**: The sphere centered at the origin is  
   \[
   x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2.
   \]  
   The circle is the intersection with a plane  
   \[
   ax + by + cz = d.
   \]

2. **Eliminate \(z\)**: (Assume \(c \neq 0\) for the generic case.) Solve the plane for \(z\):  
   \[
   z = \frac{d - ax - by}{c}.
   \]  
   Substitute into the sphere:  
   \[
   x^2 + y^2 + \left(\frac{d - ax - by}{c}\right)^2 = R^2.
   \]

3. **Obtain the conic**: Multiply by \(c^2\) and expand:  
   \[
   c^2(x^2+y^2) + (d - ax - by)^2 = c^2 R^2,
   \]  
   which simplifies to a quadratic equation in \(x\) and \(y\):  
   \[
   (c^2 + a^2)x^2 + (c^2 + b^2)y^2 + 2ab\,xy - 2ad\,x - 2bd\,y + (d^2 - c^2 R^2) = 0.
   \]  
   This represents a conic section. Because the original curve is closed and bounded, the conic is an ellipse (or a circle if the plane is parallel to the \(xy\)-plane).

4. **Degenerate case** \(c = 0\): The plane is vertical. The projection becomes the line segment of the line \(ax + by = d\) that lies inside the disk \(x^2 + y^2 \le R^2\).

5. **Shape analysis**:  
   - **Algebraic method**: Complete squares or use rotation/invariant techniques to bring the ellipse to standard form, giving the center, axes lengths, and orientation.  
   - **Geometric method**: Compute the circle’s radius \(r = \sqrt{R^2 - d^2/(a^2+b^2+c^2)}\) and its center \(\left(\frac{ad}{a^2+b^2+c^2},\frac{bd}{a^2+b^2+c^2}\right)\) (the projection center). The angle \(\phi\) between the plane and the \(xy\)-plane satisfies \(\cos\phi = |c|/\sqrt{a^2+b^2+c^2}\). Then the ellipse has semi‑major axis \(r\) (along the intersection line of the two planes, direction \((-b,a)\)) and semi‑minor axis \(r\cos\phi\).

\boxed{\text{The projection is an ellipse; eliminate } z \text{ from the sphere and plane equations to obtain a quadratic equation in } x \text{ and } y, \text{ then analyze the conic.}}

A town's reservoir, when full, can supply 32000 gallons per day for 50 days or 37000 gallons per day for 40 days. What is the maximum daily usage in gallons that ensures the supply never runs out?

Let \(I\) be the constant inflow rate (in gallons per day) and \(S\) be the full capacity of the reservoir (in gallons). Initially, the reservoir is full.

If water is drawn at \(U\) gallons per day, the net change in storage per day is \(I - U\). The storage after \(t\) days is \(S + (I - U)t\). It runs out when this becomes zero.

For the two given scenarios:

- With usage \(U_1 = 32000\) gal/day, the reservoir lasts 50 days:
  \[
  S + (I - 32000) \cdot 50 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S = (32000 - I) \cdot 50.
  \]

- With usage \(U_2 = 37000\) gal/day, the reservoir lasts 40 days:
  \[
  S + (I - 37000) \cdot 40 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad S = (37000 - I) \cdot 40.
  \]

Equate the two expressions for \(S\):
\[
(32000 - I) \cdot 50 = (37000 - I) \cdot 40.
\]

Expand:
\[
1,\!600,\!000 - 50I = 1,\!480,\!000 - 40I.
\]

Solve for \(I\):
\[
1,\!600,\!000 - 1,\!480,\!000 = 50I - 40I \;\Rightarrow\; 120,\!000 = 10I \;\Rightarrow\; I = 12,\!000 \text{ gal/day}.
\]

Thus the inflow rate is \(12,\!000\) gallons per day. The maximum daily usage that never depletes the reservoir is exactly this inflow rate, because if usage equals inflow, net change is zero and the reservoir remains full indefinitely. Any higher usage would eventually empty it, while lower usage also avoids depletion but is not the maximum.

Therefore, the answer is \(\boxed{12000}\).

Let \( X \) be an infinite-dimensional vector space and let \( S \) be a subspace of its dual \( X^* \). Does there exist a subspace \( E \) of \( X \) such that \( S \) is the annihilator of \( E \)?

We consider an infinite-dimensional vector space \(X\) over a field \(F\) and its algebraic dual \(X^*\). For a subspace \(E \subseteq X\), its annihilator is \(E^0 = \{f \in X^* : f(x)=0 \ \forall x\in E\}\). The question asks: given a subspace \(S \subseteq X^*\), does there always exist some \(E \subseteq X\) such that \(S = E^0\)?

A necessary condition is that \(S\) must equal the double annihilator of its pre‑annihilator \(S_0 = \{x \in X : f(x)=0 \ \forall f\in S\}\). Indeed, if \(S = E^0\) for some \(E\), then \(S_0 = (E^0)_0 = \operatorname{span}E\) and therefore \((S_0)^0 = (\operatorname{span}E)^0 = E^0 = S\). Hence \(S = (S_0)^0\). In finite dimensions this always holds, but in infinite dimensions it can fail: \((S_0)^0\) is the weak-\(*\) closure of \(S\), and not every subspace of \(X^*\) is weak-\(*\) closed.

We give an explicit counterexample. Let \(X\) have a countable basis \(\{e_n\}_{n\in\mathbb N}\) (such a space exists because \(X\) is infinite-dimensional). Then \(X^*\) can be identified with the space of all functions \(\mathbb N \to F\) (any assignment on the basis extends uniquely to a linear functional). Consider the subspace \(S\) consisting of those functionals that are nonzero only on finitely many basis vectors – the “finitely supported” functionals. \(S\) is a proper subspace of \(X^*\). The pre‑annihilator \(S_0\) consists of those \(x\in X\) that are annihilated by every \(f\in S\). If \(x = \sum a_i e_i\) (finite sum) is nonzero, some coefficient \(a_j \neq 0\); taking \(f\) that is \(1\) on \(e_j\) and \(0\) elsewhere (which belongs to \(S\)) gives \(f(x)=a_j \neq 0\). Hence \(S_0 = \{0\}\). Then \((S_0)^0 = \{0\}^0 = X^*\), which is strictly larger than \(S\). Thus \(S \neq (S_0)^0\), so \(S\) cannot be the annihilator of any subspace \(E\) of \(X\).

Therefore the answer is negative: not every subspace \(S \subseteq X^*\) is an annihilator.

\boxed{\text{No}}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Given the code words
$$\square  * \otimes    \,\,\,\, \oplus \rhd	\bullet  \,\,\,\, *  \square \bullet  \,\,\,\, \otimes \oslash \oplus$$
corresponding to the words
$$AMO \,\,\,\, SUR \,\,\,\, REO \,\,\,\, MAS,$$
decrypt the sequence
$$\otimes \oslash \square * \oplus \rhd	\square \bullet \otimes.$$

We are given four code words (each a triple of symbols) and four words (each a triple of letters), but the correspondences are not in order. The symbols and letters each have seven distinct types, with frequencies:

- Symbols appearing twice: □, *, ⊗, ⊕, •
- Symbols appearing once: ⊳, ø
- Letters appearing twice: A, M, O, S, R
- Letters appearing once: U, E

Thus a symbol that appears twice must map to a letter that appears twice, and a once‑appearing symbol must map to a once‑appearing letter.  
⊳ and ø occur only once, so they must correspond to U and E in some order.  
⊕ appears twice, so it must be one of A, M, O, S, R.

Now examine the code words:

- C1 = □ * ⊗  
- C2 = ⊕ ⊳ •  
- C3 = * □ •  
- C4 = ⊗ ø ⊕  

The words are: AMO, SUR, REO, MAS.

Because C2 contains the once‑symbol ⊳ in position 2, the word matched to C2 must have a once‑occurring letter in position 2. Among the words, only SUR (U) and REO (E) satisfy this. Similarly, C4 contains ø in position 2, so it must match the other of SUR/REO.

Try the two possibilities:

- If C2 → SUR and C4 → REO, then from C2 we get f(⊕)=S, f(⊳)=U, f(•)=R, and from C4 we get f(⊗)=R, f(ø)=E, f(⊕)=O – conflict on f(⊕).  
- If C2 → REO and C4 → SUR, then:
  - C2 gives f(⊕)=R, f(⊳)=E, f(•)=O.
  - C4 gives f(⊗)=S, f(ø)=U, f(⊕)=R (consistent with f(⊕)=R).

Thus we have: f(⊕)=R, f(⊳)=E, f(•)=O, f(⊗)=S, f(ø)=U.

\]

Thus the general form is

We consider the equation \(x^n + y^n = z^2\) for integers \(n \ge 3\).

- If \(n\) is odd, there exist solutions. For example, take \(x = y = 2\). Then
  \[
  2^n + 2^n = 2 \cdot 2^n = 2^{n+1} = \bigl(2^{(n+1)/2}\bigr)^2,
  \]
  and since \(n\) is odd, \((n+1)/2\) is an integer, so this gives a valid solution.

- If \(n\) is even, write \(n = 2k\) with \(k \ge 2\). Assume there exists a non‑trivial integer solution with \(x, y, z \neq 0\). By dividing by \(\gcd(x,y)\) we may assume \(\gcd(x,y)=1\).

  Then
  \[
  (x^k)^2 + (y^k)^2 = z^2,
  \]
  a primitive Pythagorean triple. Hence there exist coprime integers \(p, q\) of opposite parity such that
  \[
  x^k = p^2 - q^2,\qquad y^k = 2pq,\qquad z = p^2 + q^2.
  \]
  Without loss, let \(p\) be even and \(q\) odd.

  Because \(y^k\) is a perfect \(k\)‑th power, the factorisation \(2pq\) forces
  \[
  p = 2^{kt-1} u^k,\qquad q = v^k,
  \]
  with \(t \ge 1\), \(u, v\) odd and \(\gcd(u,v)=1\).

  The two factors of \(x^k = (p-q)(p+q)\) are coprime, so each must be a \(k\)‑th power:
  \[
  p - q = r^k,\qquad p + q = s^k,
  \]
  with \(r, s\) coprime and odd. Adding these two equations gives
  \[
  s^k + r^k = 2^{kt} u^k = (2^t u)^k.
  \]
  Thus \((r,s,2^t u)\) is a non‑trivial solution of the Fermat equation \(X^k + Y^k = Z^k\).

  - If \(k \ge 3\), Fermat’s Last Theorem (proved by Wiles, but for \(k=3,4,5\) also known classically) states that no such solution exists – a contradiction.
  - If \(k = 2\) (i.e., \(n = 4\)), the equation becomes \(x^4 + y^4 = z^2\), which was proved by Fermat (infinite descent) to have no non‑zero integer solutions.

  Hence for every even \(n \ge 4\) the assumption leads to a contradiction; therefore no solutions exist.

Consequently, the equation has no solutions precisely for even exponents \(n \ge 4\).

\[
\boxed{n\text{ even, }n\ge 4}

Find all integer sequences $\{ a_n \}$ such that $2a_{n+2}=a_{n+1}+5a_{n}$.

We consider integer sequences $\{a_n\}$ (indexed by nonnegative integers) satisfying  
\[
2a_{n+2}=a_{n+1}+5a_n\qquad\text{for all }n\ge0.
\]

**Step 1. Parity condition.**  
Reduce the recurrence modulo 2:  
$2a_{n+2}\equiv a_{n+1}+5a_n\pmod2$ gives $0\equiv a_{n+1}+a_n\pmod2$, hence $a_{n+1}\equiv a_n\pmod2$.  
Thus all terms have the same parity: either all even or all odd.

**Step 2. Reduction to the odd case.**  
If all $a_n$ are even, write $a_n=2a_n'$. Substituting shows that $\{a_n'\}$ also satisfies the recurrence and consists of integers. If $\{a_n'\}$ are again all even we can repeat the division. If a nonzero solution existed, after sufficiently many divisions we would obtain a solution with all terms odd (otherwise every $a_n$ would be divisible by arbitrarily high powers of 2, forcing $a_n=0$). Hence it suffices to show that no **odd** integer solution exists except the trivial zero solution.

**Step 3. Transform to a recurrence with integer coefficients.**  
Define $b_n=2^na_n$. Since $a_n$ are integers, $b_n\in\mathbb Z$. The recurrence becomes  
\[
b_{n+2}=b_{n+1}+10b_n.
\]  
The characteristic equation is $r^2-r-10=0$ with roots  
\[
\alpha=\frac{1+\sqrt{41}}2,\qquad \beta=\frac{1-\sqrt{41}}2.
\]  
Note that $\alpha\beta=-10$ and $\alpha+\beta=1$.

**Step 4. 2‑adic properties of $\alpha$ and $\beta$.**  
Because $41\equiv1\pmod8$, $\sqrt{41}$ exists in the 2‑adic numbers $\mathbb Q_2$. In $\mathbb Q_2$ the valuations satisfy $v_2(\alpha)+v_2(\beta)=v_2(-10)=1$ and $v_2(\alpha+\beta)=v_2(1)=0$, so one root has valuation 0 (a unit) and the other valuation 1. We label them so that $v_2(\alpha)=0$ and $v_2(\beta)=1$. Then we can write $\beta=2\gamma$ with $\gamma\in\mathbb Z_2^\times$ a 2‑adic unit.

**Step 5. Represent $b_n$.**  
There exist constants $u,v\in\mathbb Q(\sqrt{41})\subset\mathbb Q_2$ (determined by the initial values) such that  
\[
b_n = u\,\alpha^n + v\,\beta^n \qquad\text{for all }n.
\]  
Since $\beta^n=2^n\gamma^n$, we have  
\[
b_n = u\,\alpha^n + v\cdot 2^n\gamma^n.
\]

**Step 6. Divisibility requirement.**  
Because $b_n=2^na_n$ and $a_n$ is an integer, $2^n\mid b_n$; i.e., $b_n\equiv0\pmod{2^n}$.  
Reducing modulo $2^n$ and using that $v\cdot2^n\gamma^n$ is a multiple of $2^n$, we obtain  
\[
u\,\alpha^n \equiv b_n \equiv 0 \pmod{2^n}.
\]  
Since $\alpha$ is a 2‑adic unit, $\alpha^n$ is also a unit, so this congruence forces  
\[
u \equiv 0 \pmod{2^n} \quad\text{for every }n\ge0.
\]  
In $\mathbb Q_2$ the only element divisible by all powers of 2 is $0$; therefore $u=0$.

**Step 7. Conclude $a_n=0$.**  
With $u=0$ we have $b_n = v\,\beta^n$. Then  
\[
b_0 = v = a_0,\qquad b_1 = v\beta = 2a_1.
\]  
Thus $a_0=v$ is an integer, and $a_1 = v\beta/2 = v\,\gamma$. But $\gamma = \beta/2 = (1-\sqrt{41})/4$ is irrational (it is not a rational number). For $a_1$ to be an integer we must have $v=0$. Hence $a_0=a_1=0$, and by induction the recurrence forces $a_n=0$ for all $n$.

**Conclusion.**  
The only integer sequence satisfying the given recurrence is the identically zero sequence.

\[
\boxed{a_n=0\ \text{for all }n}
\]

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Evaluate the integral
$$\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{x \cos(x)}{\sin^2(x) + 1} \, \mathrm{d}x.$$

We evaluate

\[
I = \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{x \cos x}{\sin^2 x + 1}\,dx.
\]

---

### Step 1. Integration by parts

Let

\[
u = x,\qquad dv = \frac{\cos x}{\sin^2 x + 1}\,dx.
\]

Since

\[
\frac{d}{dx}\arctan(\sin x) = \frac{\cos x}{1+\sin^2 x},
\]

we have \(v = \arctan(\sin x)\). Then

\[
I = \bigl[x\arctan(\sin x)\bigr]_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} - \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \arctan(\sin x)\,dx.
\]

At \(x = \frac{\pi}{2}\): \(\sin\frac{\pi}{2}=1\), \(\arctan(1)=\frac{\pi}{4}\), so the boundary term is \(\frac{\pi}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{\pi^2}{8}\). At \(x=0\) the term vanishes. Hence

\[
I = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - J, \qquad \text{where } J = \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \arctan(\sin x)\,dx.
\]

---

### Step 2. Evaluate \(J\) by differentiating a parameter

Consider for \(a\ge 0\)

\[
F(a) = \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \arctan(a\sin x)\,dx, \qquad F(0)=0,\quad F(1)=J.
\]

Differentiate under the integral:

\[
F'(a) = \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{\sin x}{1+a^2\sin^2 x}\,dx.
\]

Substitute \(u = \cos x\), \(du = -\sin x\,dx\):

\[
F'(a) = \int_1^0 \frac{-du}{1+a^2(1-u^2)} = \int_0^1 \frac{du}{1+a^2 - a^2 u^2}.
\]

The integral is elementary:

\[
F'(a) = \frac{1}{a\sqrt{1+a^2}}\,\operatorname{arctanh}\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{1+a^2}}\right)
      = \frac{1}{a\sqrt{1+a^2}}\,\ln\!\left(\sqrt{1+a^2}+a\right).
\]

Now set \(a = \sinh t\). Then \(\sqrt{1+a^2}=\cosh t\), \(da = \cosh t\,dt\), and

\[
\ln\!\left(\sqrt{1+a^2}+a\right) = \ln(\cosh t + \sinh t) = t.
\]

Thus

\[
F'(a)\,da = \frac{1}{\sinh t\cosh t}\cdot t\cdot\cosh t\,dt = \frac{t}{\sinh t}\,dt.
\]

When \(a\) runs from \(0\) to \(1\), \(t\) runs from \(0\) to \(\operatorname{arsinh}(1) = \ln(1+\sqrt{2})\). Denote

\[
L = \ln(1+\sqrt{2}).
\]

Then

\[
J = F(1) = \int_0^L \frac{t}{\sinh t}\,dt.
\]

---

### Step 3. Compute \(K = \int_0^L \frac{t}{\sinh t}\,dt\)

Use the expansion \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sinh t} = 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty e^{-(2n+1)t}\) for \(t>0\):

\[
K = 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \int_0^L t\,e^{-(2n+1)t}\,dt.
\]

The elementary integral is

\[
\int_0^L t e^{-c t}\,dt = \frac{1}{c^2}\bigl[1-(1+cL)e^{-cL}\bigr],\quad c=2n+1.
\]

Hence

\[
K = 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(2n+1)^2}\Bigl[1 - (1+(2n+1)L)e^{-(2n+1)L}\Bigr].
\]

Let \(z = e^{-L} = \sqrt{2}-1\). Then

\[
K = 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(2n+1)^2} - 2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(1+(2n+1)L)z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)^2}.
\]

The first sum is \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{(2n+1)^2} = \frac{\pi^2}{8}\), so \(2\) times it is \(\frac{\pi^2}{4}\).

For the second sum, recognise

\[
\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)^2} =: \chi_2(z),\qquad
\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^{2n+1}}{2n+1} = \operatorname{arctanh}(z).
\]

Thus

\[
\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(1+(2n+1)L)z^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)^2}
 = \chi_2(z) + L\operatorname{arctanh}(z).
\]

Now from \(z = \sqrt{2}-1\) we have

\[
\frac{1+z}{1-z} = 1+\sqrt{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \operatorname{arctanh}(z) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+\sqrt{2}) = \frac{L}{2}.
\]

Therefore

\[
K = \frac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\bigl(\chi_2(z) + \tfrac{L^2}{2}\bigr) = \frac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\chi_2(z) - L^2.
\]

---

### Step 4. Determine \(\chi_2(z)\)

We compute the same integral \(K\) in another way. First, integrate by parts in the original form:

\[
K = \int_0^L \frac{t}{\sinh t}\,dt = \bigl[t\ln(\tanh(t/2))\bigr]_0^L - \int_0^L \ln(\tanh(t/2))\,dt.
\]

As \(t\to0^+\), \(t\ln(\tanh(t/2))\to0\); at \(t=L\), \(\tanh(L/2) = \sqrt{2}-1 = z\), and \(\ln z = -L\). Hence the boundary term equals \(L\ln z = -L^2\). Thus

\[
K = -L^2 - \int_0^L \ln(\tanh(t/2))\,dt.
\]

Now set \(u = t/2\):

\[
\int_0^L \ln(\tanh(t/2))\,dt = 2\int_0^{L/2} \ln(\tanh u)\,du.
\]

Expand \(\ln(\tanh u)\):

\[
\ln(\tanh u) = -\ln\frac{1+e^{-2u}}{1-e^{-2u}} = -2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{e^{-2(2n+1)u}}{2n+1}.
\]

Integrating termwise:

\[
\int_0^{L/2} \ln(\tanh u)\,du = -2\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{2n+1}\int_0^{L/2} e^{-2(2n+1)u}\,du
= -\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1 - e^{-(2n+1)L}}{(2n+1)^2}
= -\frac{\pi^2}{8} + \chi_2(z).
\]

Consequently

\[
K = -L^2 - 2\bigl(-\tfrac{\pi^2}{8} + \chi_2(z)\bigr) = -L^2 + \tfrac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\chi_2(z). \tag{*}
\]

On the other hand, we already had

\[
K = \tfrac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\chi_2(z) - L^2, \tag{**}
\]

which is consistent. To find \(\chi_2(z)\) we use yet another expression for the same integral \(\int_0^{L/2}\ln(\tanh u)\,du\).

Substitute \(v = \tanh u\), so \(u = \operatorname{arctanh} v\), \(du = \frac{dv}{1-v^2}\). When \(u\) goes from \(0\) to \(L/2\), \(v\) goes from \(0\) to \(z\). Then

\[
\int_0^{L/2} \ln(\tanh u)\,du = \int_0^z \frac{\ln v}{1-v^2}\,dv.
\]

Expand \(\frac{1}{1-v^2} = \sum_{m=0}^\infty v^{2m}\) (since \(0\le v<1\)):

\[
\int_0^z \frac{\ln v}{1-v^2}\,dv = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \int_0^z v^{2m}\ln v\,dv.
\]

Using \(\int_0^z v^{2m}\ln v\,dv = z^{2m+1}\Bigl(\frac{\ln z}{2m+1} - \frac{1}{(2m+1)^2}\Bigr)\),

we obtain

\[
\int_0^z \frac{\ln v}{1-v^2}\,dv = \ln z \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{z^{2m+1}}{2m+1} - \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{z^{2m+1}}{(2m+1)^2}
= \ln z \cdot \operatorname{arctanh}(z) - \chi_2(z).
\]

Since \(\ln z = -L\) and \(\operatorname{arctanh}(z) = L/2\), this equals \(-\frac{L^2}{2} - \chi_2(z)\).

Equating the two expressions for \(\int_0^{L/2}\ln(\tanh u)\,du\):

\[
-\frac{\pi^2}{8} + \chi_2(z) = -\frac{L^2}{2} - \chi_2(z).
\]

Solving for \(\chi_2(z)\):

\[
2\chi_2(z) = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - \frac{L^2}{2} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\chi_2(z) = \frac{\pi^2}{16} - \frac{L^2}{4}.
\]

---

### Step 5. Final evaluation of \(I\)

Recall \(I = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - J = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - K\). Using either \((*)\) or \((**)\),

\[
K = \frac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\chi_2(z) - L^2.
\]

Substitute \(\chi_2(z)\):

\[
K = \frac{\pi^2}{4} - 2\Bigl(\frac{\pi^2}{16} - \frac{L^2}{4}\Bigr) - L^2
  = \frac{\pi^2}{4} - \frac{\pi^2}{8} + \frac{L^2}{2} - L^2
  = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - \frac{L^2}{2}.
\]

Therefore

\[
I = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - K = \frac{\pi^2}{8} - \Bigl(\frac{\pi^2}{8} - \frac{L^2}{2}\Bigr) = \frac{L^2}{2}.
\]

Since \(L = \ln(1+\sqrt{2})\), we obtain

\[
\boxed{\frac{1}{2}\left(\ln(1+\sqrt{2})\right)^{2}}.
\]

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Given n snakes, if you repeatedly pick any 2 ends (head or tail) from the 2n available ends and tie them together, what is the probability of ending up with a single long "snake"?

We start with $n$ snakes, each with two ends (head and tail), giving $2n$ ends in total. At each step, we randomly pick two free ends and tie them together. This continues until no ends remain, which occurs after exactly $n$ ties. The final configuration consists of closed loops (since all ends are tied). The question asks for the probability that the result is a single long snake, i.e., one big loop containing all $n$ original snakes.

The process is equivalent to generating a uniformly random perfect matching on the $2n$ ends. To end with a single loop, we must never close a loop prematurely; the first $n-1$ ties must always join ends from **different** components, and the final tie will then necessarily close the last component into a loop.

Let $P(n)$ be the desired probability. For $n=1$, we trivially have a loop, so $P(1)=1$.

For $n\ge 2$, consider the first tie. There are $\binom{2n}{2}=n(2n-1)$ equally likely unordered pairs of ends. Among these, exactly $n$ pairs consist of the two ends of the same snake (one per snake). Hence
\[
\text{Probability(first tie joins the same snake)} = \frac{n}{n(2n-1)} = \frac{1}{2n-1}.
\]
If this happens, a small loop is formed immediately, making a single big loop impossible. Otherwise, with probability $1-\frac{1}{2n-1} = \frac{2n-2}{2n-1}$, the first tie joins two different snakes, merging them into one longer snake (still with two free ends). After this merge, the situation is equivalent to starting anew with $n-1$ snakes, because the remaining free ends are uniformly distributed over all possible configurations. Therefore,
\[
P(n) = \frac{2n-2}{2n-1}\, P(n-1).
\]

Iterating this recurrence with $P(1)=1$ gives
\[
P(n) = \prod_{k=2}^{n} \frac{2k-2}{2k-1}.
\]

The numerator is the product of even numbers from $2$ to $2n-2$, which equals $(2n-2)!! = 2^{n-1}(n-1)!$. The denominator is the product of odd numbers from $3$ to $2n-1$, which is $(2n-1)!!$ (since $(2n-1)!! = 1\cdot3\cdot5\cdots(2n-1)$ and the factor $1$ does not affect the product). Thus
\[
P(n) = \frac{(2n-2)!!}{(2n-1)!!} = \frac{2^{n-1}(n-1)!}{(2n-1)!!}.
\]

For verification: $n=2$ gives $\frac{2}{3}$, $n=3$ gives $\frac{8}{15}$, and $n=1$ gives $1$, matching direct enumeration.

Therefore, the probability of ending with a single long snake is
\[
\boxed{\frac{2^{n-1}(n-1)!}{(2n-1)!!}}.
\]

Each cell of a $100 \times 100$ board is colored white or blue. A cell is called "equilibrium" if among its neighboring cells there are equally many white and blue cells (cells that have a common side are considered neighboring cells). Is it possible to color the board so that there are more than 6000 equilibrium cells on it? What is the maximum number of equilibrium cells that can be on such a board?

We consider a $100 \times 100$ board. A cell's neighbors are those sharing a side. The number of neighbors depends on its position:

- Corner cells have 2 neighbors.
- Edge (non-corner) cells have 3 neighbors.
- Interior cells have 4 neighbors.

For a cell to be equilibrium, the numbers of white and blue among its neighbors must be equal. Since an odd number cannot be split into two equal integers, cells with 3 neighbors can never be equilibrium. Therefore only corners (2 neighbors) and interior cells (4 neighbors) can possibly be equilibrium.

There are $100 \times 100 = 10000$ cells total. The interior cells are those with rows $2$ to $99$ and columns $2$ to $99$, which total $98 \times 98 = 9604$. The four corner cells also have an even number of neighbors. Thus the maximum possible number of equilibrium cells cannot exceed $9604 + 4 = 9608$.

Now we exhibit a coloring that achieves this bound. Color the board in alternating rows: all cells in odd rows white, all cells in even rows blue (or the opposite). For an interior cell:

- If it lies in an odd row (white), its left and right neighbors are in the same odd row, hence white; its up and down neighbors are in even rows, hence blue. So it has exactly two white and two blue neighbors.
- If it lies in an even row (blue), its left and right neighbors are blue; up and down neighbors are white. Again two of each.

Thus every interior cell is equilibrium. For a corner, say top‑left $(1,1)$ (odd row, white). Its right neighbor $(1,2)$ is white; its down neighbor $(2,1)$ is blue. Hence one white and one blue, satisfying the equilibrium condition. The other three corners are similarly equilibrium. All edge cells have three neighbors and cannot be equilibrium. Consequently this coloring gives $9604 + 4 = 9608$ equilibrium cells.

Since the bound $9608$ is attained and no cell with an odd number of neighbors can ever be equilibrium, $9608$ is the maximum.

Therefore the answer to the first question is yes (more than $6000$ is possible), and the maximum number is $\boxed{9608}$.

\[

\[

Consequently the original inequality is true for all triangles iff \(K\ge 0\). The smallest such constant (the strongest inequality) is \(K=0\), and it is sharp because equality is attained for an equilateral triangle.

Unless otherwise stated, \( H \) is taken as a trivial \( G \)-module.  Since cohomology is additive in the coefficient module,

Thus it suffices to compute \( H^{3}(G, \mathbb{Z}_{2}) \) with trivial action.

For \( G = \mathbb{Z}_{2} \times \mathbb{Z}_{2} \), the cohomology ring with coefficients in the field \( \mathbb{F}_{2} \cong \mathbb{Z}_{2} \) is well known:

H^{*}(G, \mathbb{F}_{2}) \cong \mathbb{F}_{2}[x, y] ,

\[
c^2 = x^2 + h^2, \qquad b^2 = (a - x)^2 + h^2,
\]
where \(h = AD\). Substituting \(b = x\) gives
\[
x^2 = (a - x)^2 + h^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad h^2 = x^2 - (a - x)^2 = 2ax - a^2.
\]
Then
\[
c^2 = h^2 + x^2 = (2ax - a^2) + x^2 = x^2 + 2ax - a^2. \tag{1}
\]

Because the altitude is interior, we also need \(h^2 > 0\), so \(2x > a\) i.e. \(x > a/2\). Together with \(0 < x < a\), we have
\[

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Let \(a, b, c > 0\) and \(a(b^2 + c^2) = b + c\). Find the minimum value of the expression:
\[ P = \frac{1}{(1+a)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+b)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+c)^2} + \frac{4}{(1+a)(1+b)(1+c)}. \]

We are given \(a,b,c > 0\) and the condition \(a(b^2 + c^2) = b + c\).  
We need the minimum value of  

\[
P = \frac{1}{(1+a)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+b)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+c)^2} + \frac{4}{(1+a)(1+b)(1+c)}.
\]

---

### Step 1. Express \(a\) in terms of \(b\) and \(c\)

From the condition,
\[
a = \frac{b + c}{b^2 + c^2}.
\]

Let \(s = b + c\) and \(p = bc\). Then  
\(b^2 + c^2 = s^2 - 2p\), so  

\[
a = \frac{s}{s^2 - 2p}.
\]

Set  

\[
U = s^2 - 2p,\qquad V = 1 + s + p.
\]

Clearly \(U>0\), \(V>0\).

---

### Step 2. Write \(P\) using \(s\) and \(p\)

Compute each part:

- \(1+a = 1 + \frac{s}{U} = \frac{U+s}{U}\), so  
  \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{(1+a)^2} = \frac{U^2}{(U+s)^2}\).

- \(\displaystyle \frac{1}{(1+b)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+c)^2} = \frac{(b+c)^2 + 2(b+c) + 2 - 2bc}{(1+b)^2(1+c)^2}\)  
  (by expanding), and since \((1+b)(1+c) = V\), we get  

  \[
  \frac{1}{(1+b)^2} + \frac{1}{(1+c)^2}
  = \frac{s^2 + 2s + 2 - 2p}{V^2}
  = \frac{U + 2s + 2}{V^2}.
  \]

- \(\displaystyle \frac{4}{(1+a)(1+b)(1+c)} = \frac{4}{\frac{U+s}{U}\cdot V}
  = \frac{4U}{(U+s)V}.\)

Therefore  

\[
P = \frac{U^2}{(U+s)^2} + \frac{U+2s+2}{V^2} + \frac{4U}{(U+s)V}.
\tag{1}
\]

---

### Step 3. Show that for fixed \(s\), \(P\) decreases when \(p\) increases (i.e., when \(U\) decreases and \(V\) increases)

We treat \(s\) as a constant and consider \(P\) as a function of \(U\) and \(V\), which themselves depend on \(p\):

\[
\frac{dU}{dp} = -2,\qquad \frac{dV}{dp} = 1.
\]

By the chain rule,  

\[
\frac{dP}{dp} = -2\,\frac{\partial P}{\partial U} + \frac{\partial P}{\partial V}.
\]

From (1) compute the partial derivatives (keeping the other variable fixed):

\[
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial P}{\partial U}
&= \frac{2Us}{(U+s)^3} + \frac{1}{V^2} + \frac{4s}{(U+s)^2 V}, \\[6pt]
\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}
&= -\frac{2(U+2s+2)}{V^3} - \frac{4U}{(U+s)V^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Thus  

\[
\begin{aligned}
\frac{dP}{dp}
&= -2\left(\frac{2Us}{(U+s)^3} + \frac{1}{V^2} + \frac{4s}{(U+s)^2 V}\right)
   + \left(-\frac{2(U+2s+2)}{V^3} - \frac{4U}{(U+s)V^2}\right) \\[6pt]
&= -\frac{4Us}{(U+s)^3} - \frac{2}{V^2} - \frac{8s}{(U+s)^2 V}
   - \frac{2(U+2s+2)}{V^3} - \frac{4U}{(U+s)V^2}.
\end{aligned}
\]

All terms are negative because \(U,s,V > 0\). Hence  

\[
\frac{dP}{dp} < 0 \quad \text{for all admissible } p.
\]

Consequently, for a given sum \(s = b+c\), the value of \(P\) **decreases** when the product \(p = bc\) **increases**.  
The maximum possible product for fixed \(s\) is attained when \(b = c = \dfrac{s}{2}\) (by AM–GM). Therefore, for each \(s\), the minimum of \(P\) is achieved at \(b = c\).

---

### Step 4. Reduce to one variable

Set \(b = c = x > 0\). Then \(s = 2x\) and  

\[
a = \frac{2x}{(2x)^2/2} = \frac{1}{x}.
\]

Now substitute into \(P\):

\[
\begin{aligned}
P &= \frac{1}{(1+1/x)^2} + \frac{2}{(1+x)^2} + \frac{4}{(1+1/x)(1+x)^2} \\[6pt]
&= \left(\frac{x}{x+1}\right)^2 + \frac{2}{(x+1)^2} + \frac{4x}{(x+1)^3} \\[6pt]
&= \frac{x^2+2}{(x+1)^2} + \frac{4x}{(x+1)^3} \\[6pt]
&= \frac{(x^2+2)(x+1) + 4x}{(x+1)^3} \\[6pt]
&= \frac{x^3 + x^2 + 6x + 2}{(x+1)^3}.
\end{aligned}
\]

Define  

\[
f(x) = \frac{x^3 + x^2 + 6x + 2}{(x+1)^3},\qquad x > 0.
\]

---

### Step 5. Minimize \(f(x)\)

Compute the derivative:

\[
f'(x) = \frac{(3x^2+2x+6)(x+1)^3 - (x^3+x^2+6x+2)\cdot 3(x+1)^2}{(x+1)^6}.
\]

Factor \((x+1)^2\) from the numerator:

\[
f'(x) = \frac{(x+1)^2\bigl[(3x^2+2x+6)(x+1) - 3(x^3+x^2+6x+2)\bigr]}{(x+1)^6}.
\]

Simplify the bracket:

\[
\begin{aligned}
(3x^2+2x+6)(x+1) &= 3x^3 + 5x^2 + 8x + 6, \\
3(x^3+x^2+6x+2) &= 3x^3 + 3x^2 + 18x + 6.
\end{aligned}
\]

Subtraction gives \(2x^2 - 10x = 2x(x-5)\). Hence  

\[
f'(x) = \frac{2x(x-5)}{(x+1)^4}.
\]

Thus \(f'(x) = 0\) at \(x = 5\) (since \(x>0\)).  
- For \(0 < x < 5\), \(f'(x) < 0\) ⇒ \(f\) decreases.  
- For \(x > 5\), \(f'(x) > 0\) ⇒ \(f\) increases.

Therefore \(f\) attains its minimum at \(x = 5\).  

The minimum value is  

\[
f(5) = \frac{5^3 + 5^2 + 6\cdot5 + 2}{(5+1)^3} = \frac{125 + 25 + 30 + 2}{6^3} = \frac{182}{216} = \frac{91}{108}.
\]

---

### Conclusion

The minimum possible value of \(P\) under the given condition is \(\boxed{\dfrac{91}{108}}\).

Because the chord lengths are equal,

2R\sin\frac{\alpha}{2} = 2R\sin\frac{\beta}{2}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad \sin\frac{\alpha}{2}=\sin\frac{\beta}{2}.

Since \(0<\alpha,\beta<2\pi\) and \(A,B,C\) are distinct with \(\alpha+\beta<2\pi\), both \(\frac{\alpha}{2}\) and \(\frac{\beta}{2}\) lie in \((0,\pi)\). \]

which is also acute.  (The point \(Q\), the foot of the perpendicular from \(B\) to \(AP\), does not affect these conclusions.)

Determine if the subset \( X = \{z: |z| \le 1\} \cup \{z: |z-2| < 1\} \) of \(\mathbb{C}\) is connected. If not, identify its components.

We need to determine whether \(X = \{z: |z| \le 1\} \cup \{z: |z-2| < 1\}\) is connected.

**Step 1: Understand the two parts.**
- \(A = \{z: |z| \le 1\}\) is the closed unit disk centered at \(0\). It is convex, hence path-connected and connected.
- \(B = \{z: |z-2| < 1\}\) is the open disk centered at \(2\) with radius \(1\). It is also convex, so path-connected and connected.

**Step 2: Check intersection and closure.**
The two disks are disjoint because if \(z\) satisfied both \(|z|\le1\) and \(|z-2|<1\), then by the triangle inequality
\(|z| + |z-2| \ge |0-2| = 2\), but the left side would be \(\le 1 + (<1) < 2\), impossible.  
However, the closure of \(B\) is \(\overline{B} = \{z: |z-2| \le 1\}\). The point \(z=1\) lies in both \(A\) and \(\overline{B}\) because \(|1|=1\) and \(|1-2|=1\). Thus \(A \cap \overline{B} \ne \varnothing\).

**Step 3: Apply a general theorem.**
If two connected subsets \(A\) and \(B\) of a topological space satisfy \(A \cap \overline{B} \ne \varnothing\) (or \(\overline{A} \cap B \ne \varnothing\)), then their union is connected.  
Proof sketch: Assume a separation by two relatively open disjoint subsets of \(X = A \cup B\). By connectedness, \(A\) must lie entirely in one and \(B\) in the other. Lifting to open sets in \(\mathbb{C}\) leads to a contradiction because the open set containing \(A\) would be disjoint from \(B\) and hence from \(\overline{B}\), contradicting \(A \cap \overline{B} \ne \varnothing\).  
Therefore \(X\) is connected.

**Step 4: Path‑connectedness (alternative direct argument).**
One can also exhibit a continuous path between any two points of \(X\), proving path‑connectedness (which implies connectedness). For example, take the straight line segment from \(0\) to \(2\):
- For \(t \in [0,1]\), let \(z(t) = 2t\).  
- If \(0 \le 2t \le 1\) (i.e., \(0 \le t \le 0.5\)), then \(|z(t)| = 2t \le 1\), so \(z(t) \in A\).  
- If \(1 < 2t \le 2\) (i.e., \(0.5 < t \le 1\)), then \(|z(t)-2| = 2 - 2t < 1\), so \(z(t) \in B\).  
Thus the whole segment \([0,2]\) lies in \(X\). Any point in \(A\) can be joined to \(0\) within \(A\) (convex), any point in \(B\) can be joined to \(2\) within \(B\) (convex), and concatenation yields a path between arbitrary points. Hence \(X\) is path‑connected.

**Conclusion:** \(X\) is connected; it has only one component, namely \(X\) itself.

\[
\boxed{\text{The set } X \text{ is connected.}}

Evaluate the double integral \(\int_{2}^{\frac{2}{3}}\int_y^{2-2y}(x+2y)e^{y-x} \, dx \, dy\).

\[

### Step 1: Inner integral (with respect to \(x\))

For a fixed \(y\), set \(u = x - y\), so \(x = u + y\), \(dx = du\). When \(x = y\), \(u = 0\); when \(x = 2-2y\), \(u = 2-3y\). Then

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_{y}^{2-2y} (x+2y) e^{y-x} \, dx
&= \int_{0}^{2-3y} \bigl((u+y)+2y\bigr) e^{-u} \, du \\
&= \int_{0}^{2-3y} (u + 3y) e^{-u} \, du.
\end{aligned}
\]

Find an antiderivative:

\[
\int (u+3y)e^{-u}\,du = -(u+1+3y)e^{-u} + C.
\]

Thus

\[
\begin{aligned}
\int_{0}^{2-3y} (u+3y)e^{-u}\,du
&= \Bigl[-(u+1+3y)e^{-u}\Bigr]_{0}^{2-3y} \\
&= -\bigl(2-3y+1+3y\bigr)e^{-(2-3y)} + (1+3y) \\
&= -3\,e^{-(2-3y)} + (1+3y).
\end{aligned}
\]

Since \(e^{-(2-3y)} = e^{3y-2}\), we write

\[
I(y) = 1 + 3y - 3 e^{3y-2}.
\]

(This expression is valid for any real \(y\).)

Now

\[
I = \int_{2}^{\frac{2}{3}} \bigl(1 + 3y - 3 e^{3y-2}\bigr) \, dy.
\]

Find an antiderivative \(F(y)\):

\[
F(y) = \int (1+3y)\,dy - 3\int e^{3y-2}\,dy = y + \frac{3}{2}y^{2} - e^{3y-2}.
\]

(Check: derivative gives \(1+3y - 3e^{3y-2}\).)

Then by the fundamental theorem,

\[
I = F\left(\frac{2}{3}\right) - F(2).
\]

Compute the two values:

\[
\begin{aligned}
F\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)
&= \frac{2}{3} + \frac{3}{2}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^{2} - e^{3\cdot\frac{2}{3}-2} \\
&= \frac{2}{3} + \frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{4}{9} - e^{2-2} \\

&= \frac{2}{3} + \frac{2}{3} - 1 = \frac{4}{3} - 1 = \frac{1}{3}.
\end{aligned}
\]

\[

F(2)

&= 2 + 6 - e^{4} = 8 - e^{4}.
\end{aligned}
\]

\[
I = \frac{1}{3} - (8 - e^{4}) = e^{4} - 8 + \frac{1}{3} = e^{4} - \frac{23}{3}.

\boxed{e^{4} - \dfrac{23}{3}}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

In the $xyz$-space, let point $P$ move on the perimeter of a unit circle on the $xy$-plane centered at the origin, and let point $Q$ move on the perimeter of a circle on the $xz$-plane with radius 1 centered at $(0, 0, \sqrt{3})$. Find the maximum length of the line segment $PQ$ and the coordinates of $P$ and $Q$ that give this length.

We parametrize the points:

- $P$ lies on the unit circle in the $xy$-plane: $P = (\cos\alpha,\ \sin\alpha,\ 0)$ with $\alpha\in[0,2\pi)$.
- $Q$ lies on the circle of radius $1$ in the $xz$-plane centered at $(0,0,\sqrt3)$: $Q = (\cos\beta,\ 0,\ \sqrt3+\sin\beta)$ with $\beta\in[0,2\pi)$.

The squared distance is
\[
\begin{aligned}
d^2 &= (\cos\alpha-\cos\beta)^2 + \sin^2\alpha + (\sqrt3+\sin\beta)^2 \\
&= \cos^2\alpha -2\cos\alpha\cos\beta +\cos^2\beta +\sin^2\alpha +3+2\sqrt3\sin\beta+\sin^2\beta \\
&= 1 -2\cos\alpha\cos\beta +\cos^2\beta+3+2\sqrt3\sin\beta+\sin^2\beta \\
&= 5 + 2\sqrt3\sin\beta -2\cos\alpha\cos\beta.
\end{aligned}
\]

For a fixed $\beta$, the maximum over $\alpha$ occurs when $-2\cos\alpha\cos\beta$ is maximized. Since $\cos\alpha\in[-1,1]$, the maximum value of $-2\cos\alpha\cos\beta$ is $2|\cos\beta|$, achieved by choosing $\cos\alpha = -\operatorname{sgn}(\cos\beta)$ (i.e., $\cos\alpha=-1$ if $\cos\beta>0$ and $\cos\alpha=1$ if $\cos\beta<0$). Thus
\[
\max_\alpha d^2 = 5 + 2\sqrt3\sin\beta + 2|\cos\beta|.
\]

Now we maximise $g(\beta)=5 + 2\sqrt3\sin\beta + 2|\cos\beta|$ over $\beta$.

- When $\cos\beta\ge 0$: $g_1(\beta)=5 + 2\sqrt3\sin\beta + 2\cos\beta = 5 + 2\bigl(\sqrt3\sin\beta+\cos\beta\bigr) = 5+4\sin\!\left(\beta+\frac\pi6\right)$, because $\sqrt3\sin\beta+\cos\beta = 2\sin(\beta+\pi/6)$. Its maximum is $5+4\cdot1=9$, attained when $\sin(\beta+\pi/6)=1$, i.e., $\beta+\pi/6 = \pi/2+2k\pi \Rightarrow \beta=\pi/3+2k\pi$. For such $\beta$, $\cos\beta=1/2>0$, and we need $\cos\alpha=-1$ (i.e., $\alpha=\pi+2k\pi$). Then $P=(\cos\pi,\sin\pi,0)=(-1,0,0)$ and $Q=(\cos(\pi/3),\,0,\,\sqrt3+\sin(\pi/3))=\bigl(\frac12,\,0,\,\frac{3\sqrt3}{2}\bigr)$.

Determine all integers $k$ such that the equation
$$\frac{1}{x}+\frac{1}{y}+\frac{1}{z}=\frac{k}{xyz}$$
has an infinite number of integer solutions $(x,y,z)$ with $\gcd(k,xyz)=1$.

The given equation simplifies to $xy + yz + zx = k$. The condition $\gcd(k, xyz)=1$ implies that for any prime factor $p$ of $k$, none of $x,y,z$ is divisible by $p$. In particular, if $k$ is even, then $2\mid k$ forces $xyz$ to be odd, so $x,y,z$ are all odd. But the sum of three odd numbers $xy+yz+zx$ is odd, contradicting $k$ even. Hence no solutions exist for even $k$, and $k=0$ also fails (it forces $|xyz|=1$, leading to parity contradiction).

Now assume $k$ is odd. For any integer $y$, set $z = 1-y$ and $x = y^2 + k - y$. Then a direct check shows $xy+yz+zx = k$, and $x,y,z$ are integers with $x,y,z \neq 0$ provided $y\neq1$ (since $z=0$ only when $y=1$) and, because $k$ is odd, $x$ is never zero (if $x=0$ then $y^2-y+k=0$, but the left side is odd while $0$ is even). Thus for every $y\neq1$ we obtain a valid solution.

To satisfy $\gcd(k,xyz)=1$, we must avoid any prime factor $p$ of $k$ dividing $x$, $y$, or $z$. From the expressions:
- $y$ must not be $0\pmod p$.
- $z = 1-y$ must not be $0\pmod p$, i.e., $y\not\equiv1\pmod p$.
- $x = y^2 - y + k \equiv y(y-1)\pmod p$ (since $k\equiv0\pmod p$), so $x\not\equiv0\pmod p$ exactly when $y\not\equiv0,1\pmod p$.

Hence for each prime $p\mid k$ we need $y\not\equiv0,1\pmod p$. If $k$ has no prime factors ($k=\pm1$), the condition is automatically true. If $k$ has prime factors, let $P$ be the product of all distinct primes dividing $k$. Choose $y\equiv2\pmod P$ (possible by the Chinese remainder theorem). Then $y$ avoids $0$ and $1$ modulo every $p\mid k$, and $y\neq1$ because $2\not\equiv1\pmod P$ for $P>1$. For $k=\pm1$, simply pick any $y\neq1$. In all cases there are infinitely many such $y$ (e.g., $y = 2 + tP$), giving infinitely many solutions $(x,y,z)$ with $\gcd(k,xyz)=1$.

Therefore the required integers $k$ are exactly the odd integers.

\boxed{k \text{ is an odd integer}}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

For what values of $n$ and $p$ is it the case that $\lim_{k \to \infty} \frac{x_{s,k}}{k}=\frac{1}{n}$ for all songs $s$ with probability 1, where $x_{s,k}$ is the number of times $s$ has been played after the first $k$ total plays, given that the probability of a song being selected is proportional to $x^p$?

We consider a system with $n$ songs. After $k$ total plays, let $x_{s,k}$ be the number of times song $s$ has been played. The selection probability at each step is proportional to $(x_{s,k})^p$. To avoid issues with $0^p$ we assume initial counts are positive (e.g., each song has been played at least once). The goal is to determine when
\[
\lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{x_{s,k}}{k} = \frac{1}{n} \quad \text{for every song } s
\]
holds with probability $1$.

Let $X_i(k)$ be the count for song $i$ after $k$ total plays, and define the proportions $Y_i(k)=X_i(k)/k$ (for large $k$ we may use the total number of plays $N$ as an index). The update when a song $i$ is chosen is
\[
X_i(N+1)=X_i(N)+1,\qquad N\mapsto N+1.
\]
A simple algebra yields
\[
Y_i(N+1)-Y_i(N)=\frac{1}{N+1}\bigl(\xi_i(N)-Y_i(N)\bigr),
\]
where $\xi_i(N)$ is the indicator that song $i$ was chosen at that step. Conditional on the current state,
\[
\mathbb{E}[\xi_i(N)\mid\mathcal{F}_N] = \frac{X_i(N)^p}{\sum_j X_j(N)^p} = \frac{Y_i(N)^p}{\sum_j Y_j(N)^p} \equiv \phi_i(\mathbf{Y}(N)).
\]

This recursion is a stochastic approximation with step size $\gamma_N=1/(N+1)$ (which satisfies $\sum\gamma_N=\infty$, $\sum\gamma_N^2<\infty$). The associated mean ODE on the simplex $\{\mathbf{y}\ge0,\sum y_i=1\}$ is
\[
\frac{dy_i}{dt} = \phi_i(\mathbf{y}) - y_i, \qquad 
\phi_i(\mathbf{y}) = \frac{y_i^p}{\sum_j y_j^p}.
\]

We analyze this ODE.

*Case $p<1$.*  Define $u_i = y_i^{1-p}$ (note $1-p>0$).  Differentiating,
\[
\frac{du_i}{dt} = (1-p)y_i^{-p}\frac{dy_i}{dt}
= (1-p)y_i^{-p}\bigl(\phi_i(\mathbf{y})-y_i\bigr)
= (1-p)\Bigl(\frac{1}{\sum_j y_j^p} - u_i\Bigr).
\]
Hence for any $i,j$,
\[
\frac{d}{dt}(u_i-u_j) = -(1-p)(u_i-u_j),
\]
so $|u_i(t)-u_j(t)|$ decays exponentially to $0$.  Therefore all $u_i$ converge to a common value $u^*$.  Because $\sum y_i=1$ and $y_i = u_i^{1/(1-p)}$, at equilibrium we must have $n (u^*)^{1/(1-p)} = 1$, i.e. $u^* = (1/n)^{1-p}$ and consequently $y_i\to 1/n$.  The point $(1/n,\dots,1/n)$ is globally asymptotically stable in the interior.

Stochastic approximation theory (Robbins–Monro) then implies that, under mild conditions (the function $\phi(\mathbf{y})-\mathbf{y}$ is Lipschitz on compact subsets of the interior and the martingale noise has bounded variance), the stochastic process $\mathbf{Y}(k)$ converges almost surely to this stable equilibrium.  Hence for $p<1$ we obtain the desired limit with probability $1$.

*Case $p=1$.*  Then $\phi_i(\mathbf{y})=y_i$, so the ODE becomes $dy_i/dt = 0$; every state is an equilibrium.  In the actual stochastic process the proportions form a martingale and converge almost surely to a random limit having a Dirichlet distribution (unless $n=1$).  This limit is not deterministic $1/n$ in general; therefore $p=1$ does not satisfy the condition.

*Case $p>1$.*  The same computation gives $d(u_i-u_j)/dt = -(1-p)(u_i-u_j)$ with $-(1-p)=p-1>0$, so differences grow exponentially.  The interior equilibrium becomes unstable and trajectories approach the boundary where one song dominates (winner‑takes‑all).  Consequently $\lim x_{s,k}/k$ cannot equal $1/n$ for all songs.

For $n=1$ the statement holds trivially for any $p$, but for $n\ge2$ the analysis above shows that the required almost‑sure convergence occurs precisely when $p<1$.

Thus the answer is:
\[
\boxed{p<1}
\]

For the equation to hold, at least two of these must have the same smallest exponent (so that they can balance) and the third must be of higher order.  The three pairwise equalities are:

* \(5=6a\) \(\Rightarrow a=5/6\); then exponents: \(A=5\), \(B=2+2a=11/3<5\), so \(B\) has the smallest exponent alone → impossible.
* \(5=2+2a\) \(\Rightarrow a=3/2\); then exponents: \(A=B=5\), \(C=9>5\).  The left‑hand side would be \(\mathcal{O}(x^{5})\) while the right‑hand side is \(\mathcal{O}(x^{9})\), so equality cannot hold because the left‑hand side is positive.
* \(2+2a=6a\) \(\Rightarrow a=1/2\); then exponents: \(B=C=3\), \(A=5>3\).  This gives a consistent balance.

Thus the only possible leading behaviour is \(a=1/2\).  Write \(y=x^{1/2}u\).  Substituting into the equation yields

\[
x^{5}+x^{2}(x^{1/2}u)^{2}=(x^{1/2}u)^{6}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad x^{5}+x^{3}u^{2}=x^{3}u^{6}.
\]

Dividing by \(x^{3}\) (valid for \(x\neq0\)) we obtain

\[
x^{2}+u^{2}=u^{6}\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad u^{6}-u^{2}=x^{2}.
\]

As \(x\to0\), \(u\) must approach a root of \(u^{6}-u^{2}=0\), i.e. \(u=0\) or \(u=\pm1\).  If \(u\to0\) then the left‑hand side \(u^{6}-u^{2}\approx -u^{2}<0\) while the right‑hand side \(x^{2}>0\) – impossible.  Hence \(u\to\pm1\).

Take \(u=1+w\) with \(w\) small.  Expanding gives

\[
(1+w)^{6}-(1+w)^{2}=4w+14w^{2}+20w^{3}+15w^{4}+6w^{5}+w^{6}=x^{2}.
\]

Because the linear term is non‑zero, \(w\) can be expanded as a power series in \(x^{2}\).  Substituting \(w=c_{1}x^{2}+c_{2}x^{4}+\cdots\) and solving yields

\[
c_{1}=\frac{1}{4},\qquad c_{2}=-\frac{7}{32},\qquad\ldots
\]

Thus

\[
u=1+\frac{1}{4}x^{2}-\frac{7}{32}x^{4}+O(x^{6}),
\]

and therefore

\[
y=x^{1/2}\left(1+\frac{1}{4}x^{2}-\frac{7}{32}x^{4}+O(x^{6})\right)=\sqrt{x}+\frac{1}{4}x^{5/2}-\frac{7}{32}x^{9/2}+O(x^{13/2}).
\]

For the branch with \(u\to-1\), writing \(u=-1+v\) gives an analogous expansion

\[
v=-\frac{1}{4}x^{2}+\frac{7}{32}x^{4}+O(x^{6}),
\]

so that

\[
y=-\sqrt{x}-\frac{1}{4}x^{5/2}+\frac{7}{32}x^{9/2}+O(x^{13/2}).
\]

Both branches are real for \(x>0\) and are the only solutions that tend to zero as \(x\to0^{+}\).

\[
\boxed{y=\pm\sqrt{x}\left(1+\frac{1}{4}x^{2}-\frac{7}{32}x^{4}+O(x^{6})\right)\ \text{as}\ x\to0^{+}}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Find the minimum of $\int_0^1 f$ over all continuous functions $f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}$ that satisfy $f(x)+f(y)\geq |x-y|$ for all $x,y\in [0,1]$.

We aim to minimize \(\displaystyle I = \int_0^1 f(x)\,dx\) over continuous functions \(f:[0,1]\to\mathbb{R}\) satisfying  
\[
f(x)+f(y) \ge |x-y|\qquad\text{for all }x,y\in[0,1].
\]

\section*{Lower bound via a dual measure}
Consider the measure \(\pi\) on \([0,1]^2\) defined by  
\[
\pi(B) = \frac12 \int_0^1 \mathbf{1}_B\bigl(x,1-x\bigr)\,dx
\]
for any Borel set \(B\). Clearly \(\pi\ge 0\). Its first marginal is  
\[
\pi_1(A)=\pi(A\times[0,1])=\frac12\int_0^1\mathbf{1}_A(x)\,dx=\frac12|A|,
\]
and similarly the second marginal is \(\pi_2(A)=\frac12|A|\). Hence  
\[
(\pi_1+\pi_2)(A)=|A|,
\]
i.e., \(\pi_1+\pi_2\) equals Lebesgue measure on \([0,1]\).

For any admissible \(f\), the inequality \(f(x)+f(y)\ge|x-y|\) holds for all \((x,y)\). Integrating with respect to \(\pi\) gives  
\[
\int (f(x)+f(y))\,d\pi(x,y) \ge \int |x-y|\,d\pi(x,y).
\]
The left‑hand side equals \(\int f\,d(\pi_1+\pi_2)=\int_0^1 f(x)\,dx\) because \(\pi_1+\pi_2\) is Lebesgue measure. Therefore  
\[
I \ge \int_{[0,1]^2}|x-y|\,d\pi.
\]
Now compute the right‑hand side:
\[
\int |x-y|\,d\pi = \frac12\int_0^1\bigl|x-(1-x)\bigr|\,dx = \frac12\int_0^1|2x-1|\,dx.
\]
Since \(\displaystyle\int_0^1|2x-1|\,dx = \frac12\), we obtain  
\[
I \ge \frac12\cdot\frac12 = \frac14.
\]
Thus every admissible \(f\) satisfies \(\displaystyle\int_0^1 f\ge \frac14\).

\section*{Attainment of the bound}
Define \(f(x)=|x-\tfrac12|\). This function is continuous on \([0,1]\). For any \(x,y\) we have by the triangle inequality  
\[
|x-y| = \bigl|(x-\tfrac12)-(y-\tfrac12)\bigr| \le |x-\tfrac12|+|y-\tfrac12| = f(x)+f(y),
\]
so \(f\) is admissible. Its integral is  
\[
\int_0^1|x-\tfrac12|\,dx = 2\int_0^{1/2}\Bigl(\frac12-x\Bigr)\,dx = 2\Bigl[\frac{x}{2}-\frac{x^2}{2}\Bigr]_0^{1/2} = 2\cdot\frac18 = \frac14.
\]

Hence the lower bound \(\frac14\) is achieved. Therefore the minimum possible value of the integral is \(\frac14\).

\[
\boxed{\frac{1}{4}}

Given an algebraic field extension \( K \leq F \), a polynomial \( f(x) \in F[X] \), and knowing the degree of the extension \([F: K]\) and the degree of \( f \), find a bound for the degree of \( f \cdot g \).

We are given a finite algebraic field extension \(K \leq F\) with degree \([F:K] = d\) and a polynomial \(f(x) \in F[X]\) of degree \(m = \deg f\). We want a bound for the degree of the product \(f \cdot g\) for a suitably chosen nonzero polynomial \(g \in F[X]\) such that \(f g \in K[X]\). (The problem implicitly asks for the maximal possible degree of such a product, or at least an upper bound that depends only on \(d\) and \(m\).)

A standard approach uses linear algebra. Write \(f(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{m} a_i x^i\) with \(a_i \in F\) and let \(g(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{t} b_j x^j\) with unknown coefficients \(b_j \in F\). The coefficients of \(h(x) = f(x)g(x)\) are
\[
c_k = \sum_{i+j=k} a_i b_j.
\]
We require \(c_k \in K\) for all \(k\). Treat \(F\) as a \(d\)-dimensional \(K\)-vector space; fix a basis \(\{e_1,\dots,e_d\}\) with \(e_1 = 1\). Write each \(a_i = \sum_{s=1}^{d} \alpha_{i,s} e_s\) and each \(b_j = \sum_{r=1}^{d} \beta_{j,r} e_r\) with \(\alpha_{i,s}, \beta_{j,r} \in K\). Substituting and using the multiplication constants of the basis, the condition \(c_k \in K\) translates to a homogeneous linear system over \(K\) in the unknowns \(\beta_{j,r}\).

The number of unknowns is \((t+1)d\). The number of equations (the conditions that the coefficients of \(e_2,\dots,e_d\) vanish for each \(c_k\)) is \((m+t+1)(d-1)\). To guarantee a nonzero solution we need
\[
(t+1)d > (m+t+1)(d-1).
\]
Solving this inequality:
\[
(t+1)d > (m+t+1)(d-1) \;\Longrightarrow\; td + d > (d-1)m + (d-1)t + (d-1)
\]
\[
\Longrightarrow\; t\bigl(d-(d-1)\bigr) > (d-1)m + (d-1) - d
\]
\[
\Longrightarrow\; t > (d-1)m - 1.
\]
Since \(t\) is an integer, the smallest possible choice is \(t = (d-1)m\). With this choice the number of unknowns exceeds the number of equations by exactly \(1\), so a nonzero solution exists. Hence we can find a nonzero polynomial \(g\) with \(\deg g \le (d-1)m\) such that \(f g \in K[X]\). Consequently,
\[
\deg(f g) = \deg f + \deg g \le m + (d-1)m = d\,m = [F:K]\cdot\deg f.
\]

Thus the desired bound is \(\boxed{[F:K] \cdot \deg f}\).

Find the minimum value of the expression $\frac{1}{a} + \frac{2}{b} + \frac{3}{c}$ where $a, b, c$ are positive real numbers such that $21ab + 2bc + 8ca \leq 12$.

We need to minimize \(S = \frac{1}{a} + \frac{2}{b} + \frac{3}{c}\) for positive real numbers \(a,b,c\) satisfying \(21ab + 2bc + 8ca \le 12\).

**Step 1: Reduction to equality case.**  
If \(21ab + 2bc + 8ca < 12\), we can scale \((a,b,c)\) by a factor \(t>1\) to \((ta,tb,tc)\). Then the left‑hand side becomes \(t^2(21ab+2bc+8ca)\), which increases, while \(S\) becomes \(\frac{1}{ta}+\frac{2}{tb}+\frac{3}{tc}= \frac{S}{t}\), which decreases. Choosing \(t = \sqrt{12/(21ab+2bc+8ca)}>1\) makes the constraint an equality and lowers \(S\). Hence the minimum of \(S\) occurs when  
\[
21ab + 2bc + 8ca = 12.
\]

**Step 2: Homogeneity and the invariant.**  
\(S\) is homogeneous of degree \(-1\), and the constraint expression is homogeneous of degree \(2\). Therefore \(S^2(21ab+2bc+8ca)\) is homogeneous of degree \(0\); its value does not change if we multiply all variables by the same factor. For any triple with \(21ab+2bc+8ca=12\), we have  
\[
S = \sqrt{\frac{Q}{12}}, \qquad \text{where } Q = \left(\frac{1}{a}+\frac{2}{b}+\frac{3}{c}\right)^2(21ab+2bc+8ca).
\]  
Thus minimizing \(S\) subject to the equality is equivalent to minimizing \(Q\) over all positive \(a,b,c\).

**Step 3: Using Lagrange multipliers on the equality problem.**  
We minimize \(S\) under \(g(a,b,c)=21ab+2bc+8ca=12\). The Lagrangian gives  
\[
\nabla S = \lambda \nabla g,
\]  
which yields the system  
\[
\frac{1}{a^2} = \lambda(21b+8c),\quad
\frac{2}{b^2} = \lambda(21a+2c),\quad
\frac{3}{c^2} = \lambda(2b+8a).
\]  
Because the right‑hand sides are positive, \(\lambda<0\); set \(\mu = -\lambda>0\) to obtain  
\[
\frac{1}{a^2} = \mu(21b+8c),\quad
\frac{2}{b^2} = \mu(21a+2c),\quad
\frac{3}{c^2} = \mu(2b+8a). \tag{1}
\]

**Step 4: Solving for the ratios \(a:b:c\).**  
Divide the first two equations of (1):  
\[
\frac{b^2}{2a^2} = \frac{21b+8c}{21a+2c}.
\]  
Similarly, using the first and third:  
\[
\frac{c^2}{3a^2} = \frac{21b+8c}{2b+8a}.
\]  
These relations are scale‑invariant, so we may fix \(b=1\) temporarily to find the ratios. With \(b=1\), the equations reduce to  
\[
\frac{1}{2a^2} = \frac{21+8c}{21a+2c} \quad\text{and}\quad \frac{c^2}{3a^2} = \frac{21+8c}{2+8a}.
\]  
Solving (details are given in the full reasoning) yields  
\[
a = \frac{5}{12},\qquad c = \frac{15}{8}.
\]  
Hence the ratio is  
\[
a : b : c = \frac{5}{12} : 1 : \frac{15}{8} = 10 : 24 : 45.
\]  
So the optimal direction is \((a,b,c) = (10t,\,24t,\,45t)\) for some \(t>0\).

**Step 5: Determining \(t\) from the constraint.**  
Substitute into the constraint (with equality):  
\[
21(10t)(24t) + 2(24t)(45t) + 8(45t)(10t) = (5040+2160+3600)t^2 = 10800 t^2 = 12.
\]  
Thus \(t^2 = \frac{12}{10800} = \frac{1}{900}\) and \(t = \frac{1}{30}\). Consequently,  
\[
a = \frac{10}{30} = \frac13,\quad b = \frac{24}{30} = \frac45,\quad c = \frac{45}{30} = \frac32.
\]

**Step 6: Computing the minimal value.**  
\[
S = \frac{1}{1/3} + \frac{2}{4/5} + \frac{3}{3/2} = 3 + \frac{2\cdot5}{4} + \frac{3\cdot2}{3} = 3 + \frac{10}{4} + 2 = 3 + 2.5 + 2 = 7.5 = \frac{15}{2}.
\]

**Step 7: Justification that this is the minimum.**  
The function \(S\) is continuous on the set defined by \(21ab+2bc+8ca = 12\), and it tends to \(+\infty\) as any variable tends to \(0\) or \(\infty\) while keeping the constraint (since then at least one reciprocal becomes unbounded). Therefore a global minimum exists and must occur at an interior critical point. The Lagrange multiplier system gives a unique such point (up to scaling), so the found value is the minimum.

Thus the minimum value of \(\frac{1}{a}+\frac{2}{b}+\frac{3}{c}\) under the given condition is \(\frac{15}{2}\).

\[
\boxed{\frac{15}{2}}
\]

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

A cylinder with radius $24$ and height $18$ has points $X$ and $Y$ on its top face such that the minor arc $XY$ subtends an angle of $120^\circ$. A plane slices the cylinder through $X$, $Y$, and the center of the cylinder. The area of the shape the plane cuts through is expressed as $G \sqrt{B} + F\pi$, where $G$, $B$, and $F$ are integers and $B$ is not divisible by the square of any prime. Find $G + B + F$.

The cylinder has radius \(24\) and height \(18\). Place the cylinder with its axis along the \(z\)-axis, bottom face at \(z=0\) and top face at \(z=18\). The center of the cylinder (midpoint of the axis) is \(C=(0,0,9)\). Choose coordinates so that points \(X\) and \(Y\) on the top face have coordinates \(X=(12,12\sqrt{3},18)\) and \(Y=(12,-12\sqrt{3},18)\), which gives the chord \(XY\) subtending a \(120^\circ\) central angle.

The plane through \(X\), \(Y\), and \(C\) has equation \(3x - 4z + 36 = 0\). In this plane, points satisfy \(x = \frac{4z-36}{3}\), with \(y\) free. The intersection of the plane with the solid cylinder is described by \(0 \le z \le 18\) and \(y^2 \le 576 - \left(\frac{4z-36}{3}\right)^2\).

Parameterizing the plane by \((y,z)\), the area element is \(dA = \| \mathbf{r}_y \times \mathbf{r}_z \| \, dy\,dz = \frac{5}{3}\,dy\,dz\). Thus the cross‑sectional area is

\[
A = \frac{5}{3} \int_{0}^{18} 2\sqrt{576 - \left(\frac{4z-36}{3}\right)^2}\,dz.
\]

Substitute \(u = \frac{4z-36}{3}\), so \(dz = \frac{3}{4}du\) and the limits become \(u=-12\) to \(u=12\). Then

\[
\int_{0}^{18} \sqrt{576 - \left(\frac{4z-36}{3}\right)^2}\,dz = \frac{3}{4} \int_{-12}^{12} \sqrt{576 - u^2}\,du = 108\sqrt{3} + 72\pi.
\]

Therefore

\[
A = \frac{10}{3}\bigl(108\sqrt{3} + 72\pi\bigr) = 360\sqrt{3} + 240\pi.
\]

This is of the form \(G\sqrt{B} + F\pi\) with \(G=360\), \(B=3\), \(F=240\). Hence

\[
G + B + F = 360 + 3 + 240 = 603.
\]

\boxed{603}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Find the degree of the polynomial \( f(x) = (1 - x)(1 - x^2) \cdots (1 - x^{2007}) \) modulo \( x^{2008} \).

The polynomial \(f(x) = \prod_{k=1}^{2007} (1 - x^k)\) is considered modulo \(x^{2008}\), i.e., we keep only terms of degree less than 2008. For any exponent \(d < 2008\), the coefficient of \(x^d\) in the finite product equals the coefficient in the infinite product \(\prod_{k=1}^\infty (1 - x^k)\), because all parts in a partition of \(d\) into distinct parts are at most \(d \le 2007\). By Euler’s pentagonal number theorem,
\[
\prod_{k=1}^\infty (1 - x^k) = \sum_{m=-\infty}^\infty (-1)^m x^{m(3m-1)/2} = 1 + \sum_{m=1}^\infty (-1)^m \bigl( x^{m(3m-1)/2} + x^{m(3m+1)/2} \bigr).
\]
Thus the only exponents with nonzero coefficients are the generalized pentagonal numbers \(m(3m-1)/2\) and \(m(3m+1)/2\) for \(m \ge 1\). The largest such number less than 2008 is obtained for \(m = 36\): 
\[
\frac{36 \cdot (3\cdot 36+1)}{2} = \frac{36 \cdot 109}{2} = 1962.
\]
(For \(m = 37\), both \(\frac{37\cdot 110}{2}=2035\) and \(\frac{37\cdot 112}{2}=2072\) exceed 2007.) Therefore, the highest-degree term with nonzero coefficient in the truncated polynomial is \(x^{1962}\), and the degree is \(1962\).

\boxed{1962}

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Solve the equation $x \cdot y! + 2y \cdot x! = z!$ for $x, y, z \in \mathbb{N}$.

We consider the equation

\[
x \cdot y! + 2y \cdot x! = z!,\qquad x,y,z\in\mathbb{N}
\]

with \(\mathbb{N}=\{1,2,3,\dots\}\).  

---

### Case 1: \(x \le y\)

**Subcase \(x = 1\).**  
The equation becomes \(y! + 2y = z!\).  
For \(y = 1\): \(1 + 2 = 3\) – not a factorial.  
For \(y = 2\): \(2 + 4 = 6 = 3!\) gives \((1,2,3)\).  
For \(y \ge 3\): \(y! < y!+2y < (y+1)!\) because \((y+1)! - y! = y\cdot y! > 2y\).  
Thus no other solutions.  

**Subcase \(x \ge 2\).**  
From \(x \le y\) we have  
\[
x y! \le z! = x y! + 2y x! \le x y! + 2y y! = y!(x+2y) \le 3y\,y!.
\]
Also \(z! > y!\) because \(x\ge2\).  
Now \((y+2)! = (y+2)(y+1)y! > 3y\,y!\) for all \(y\ge1\) (since \((y+2)(y+1) > 3y\)).  
Hence the only possible factorial in the interval \((y!,\,3y\,y!]\) is \((y+1)!\), so \(z = y+1\).  

Plugging \(z = y+1\) into the original equation gives  
\[
x y! + 2y x! = (y+1)! = (y+1)y!.
\]
Cancel \(y!\) (nonzero) to obtain  
\[
2y x! = (y+1-x)y!.
\]
Dividing by \(y\) yields  
\[
2 x! = (y+1-x)(y-1)!.
\tag{★}
\]
Set \(d = y - x\ge 0\).  
* If \(d = 0\): (★) becomes \(2x! = (1)(x-1)! \Rightarrow 2x = 1\) – impossible.  
* If \(d = 1\): (★) becomes \(2x! = 2\cdot x!\) – holds for all \(x\).  
* If \(d \ge 2\): then \((y+1-x) = d+1 \ge 3\) and \((y-1)! \ge (x+1)!\) (since \(y \ge x+2\)), so RHS \(\ge 3(x+1)! > 2x!\) for \(x\ge2\). No solutions.  

Thus \(d=1\), i.e. \(y = x+1\). Then \(z = y+1 = x+2\).  
Therefore all solutions with \(x \le y\) are  
\[
(x,y,z) = (n,\,n+1,\,n+2),\qquad n\in\mathbb{N}.
\]

---

### Case 2: \(x > y\)

Since \(y \le x-1\), we have \(x y! \le x (x-1)! = x!\). Hence  
\[
2y\,x! \le z! = 2y\,x! + x y! \le 2y\,x! + x! = x!(2y+1).
\]
Also \(z! \ge 2y\,x! \ge 2x!\) (because \(y\ge1\)) forces \(z \ge x+1\) (otherwise \(z!/x! \le 1\)). Thus \(k = z!/x!\) is an integer satisfying  
\[
2y \le k \le 2y+1.
\]
Hence \(k = 2y\) or \(k = 2y+1\).

* If \(k = 2y\) then \(z! = 2y\,x!\) and the original equation gives \(x y! = 0\) – impossible.  
* So \(k = 2y+1\), i.e. \(z! = (2y+1)x!\). Substituting back yields  
\[
(2y+1)x! = x y! + 2y x! \;\Longrightarrow\; x! = x y! \;\Longrightarrow\; (x-1)! = y!.
\tag{†}
\]

For positive integers, the factorial function is strictly increasing, so \((x-1)! = y!\) implies \(y = x-1\) (with \(x\ge2\)). Insert \(y = x-1\) into \(z! = (2y+1)x! = (2x-1)x!\).  

* For \(x = 2\): \((2x-1)x! = 3\cdot 2 = 6 = 3!\) – gives \((2,1,3)\).  
* For \(x \ge 3\): one checks that \((2x-1)x!\) lies strictly between \((x+1)!\) and \((x+2)!\), hence cannot be a factorial.  

Thus the only solution with \(x > y\) is \((2,1,3)\).

---

### Case 3: \(x = y\)

Then the equation becomes \(3x\,x! = z!\). For \(x\ge1\), \(3x\,x!\) is never a factorial (e.g. compare with \((x+1)! = (x+1)x!\)). No solutions.

---

Collecting all solutions (with \(\mathbb{N}=\{1,2,3,\dots\}\)) we obtain  

\[
\boxed{\{(n,\,n+1,\,n+2)\mid n\in\mathbb{N}\}\;\cup\;\{(2,1,3)\}}.
\]

Solve the following math problem. Make sure to put the answer (and only answer) inside \boxed{}.

Given that the series $\sum{a_{n}}$ diverges and $a_{n} > 0$ for all $n$, describe the behavior (convergence or divergence) of the series $\sum{\frac{a_{n}}{1 + na_{n}}}$.

We are given that \(a_n > 0\) for all \(n\) and that \(\sum a_n\) diverges. We need to determine the behavior of the series \(\sum b_n\) where \(b_n = \frac{a_n}{1 + n a_n}\).

---

### Step 1. Rewrite \(b_n\)

Let \(t = n a_n > 0\). Then  
\[
b_n = \frac{a_n}{1 + n a_n} = \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{t}{1 + t}.
\]

### Step 2. Comparison with \(\min(a_n, 1/n)\)

Notice that  
\[
\min(a_n, 1/n) = \min\left(\frac{t}{n}, \frac{1}{n}\right) = \frac{1}{n} \min(t, 1).
\]

Now compare \(\frac{t}{1+t}\) with \(\min(t,1)\):
- If \(0 < t \le 1\), then \(\min(t,1)=t\) and \(\frac{t}{1+t} \ge \frac{t}{2}\) (since \(1+t \le 2\)).
- If \(t \ge 1\), then \(\min(t,1)=1\) and \(\frac{t}{1+t} = 1 - \frac{1}{1+t} \ge 1 - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\).

Thus for all \(t > 0\),
\[
\frac{t}{1+t} \ge \frac{1}{2} \min(t,1).
\]

Also, clearly \(\frac{t}{1+t} \le \min(t,1)\) because \(\frac{t}{1+t} \le t\) and \(\frac{t}{1+t} \le 1\).

Multiplying by \(\frac{1}{n}\) gives
\[
\frac{1}{2} \min(a_n, 1/n) \le b_n \le \min(a_n, 1/n).
\]

Therefore the two series \(\sum b_n\) and \(\sum \min(a_n, 1/n)\) converge or diverge together.

### Step 3. Behavior of \(\sum \min(a_n, 1/n)\) when \(\sum a_n\) diverges

The condition \(\sum a_n\) diverges does **not** force \(\sum \min(a_n, 1/n)\) to diverge; it can either converge or diverge. Examples:

- **Divergence example**: \(a_n = \frac{1}{n}\).  
  Then \(\min(a_n, 1/n) = \frac{1}{n}\), so \(\sum \min(a_n, 1/n)\) diverges. Consequently \(\sum b_n\) diverges (indeed \(b_n = \frac{1}{2n}\)).

- **Convergence example**: Choose a sparse set of indices, e.g., \(n_k = 2^k\). Define  
  \[
  a_n = \begin{cases}
  1, & n = 2^k,\\
  \dfrac{1}{n^2}, & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
  \]  
  Then \(\sum a_n\) diverges because the infinitely many terms equal to \(1\) sum to infinity.  
  For this sequence:
  - If \(n = 2^k\), then \(a_n = 1 > \frac{1}{n}\), so \(\min(a_n, 1/n) = \frac{1}{2^k}\).  
  - For other \(n\), \(a_n = \frac{1}{n^2} \le \frac{1}{n}\), so \(\min(a_n, 1/n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\).  
  Hence \(\sum \min(a_n, 1/n)\) converges (geometric series plus a convergent \(p\)-series).  
  By the comparison above, \(\sum b_n\) also converges.

### Conclusion

The series \(\displaystyle \sum \frac{a_n}{1 + n a_n}\) may **converge** or **diverge**; its behavior is exactly the same as that of \(\displaystyle \sum \min\!\left(a_n, \frac{1}{n}\right)\). The divergence of \(\sum a_n\) alone does not determine the outcome.

\[
\boxed{\text{The series }\sum \frac{a_n}{1+na_n}\text{ may either converge or diverge; it converges iff }\sum \min\!\left(a_n,\frac{1}{n}\right)\text{ converges.}}
\]
