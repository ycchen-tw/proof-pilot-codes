## Problem

Alice the architect and Bob the builder play a game. First, Alice
 chooses two points $P$ and $Q$ in the plane and a subset $S$ of
 the plane, which are announced to Bob. Next, Bob marks infinitely
 many points in the plane, designating each a city. He may not place
 two cities within distance at most one unit of each other, and no
 three cities he places may be collinear. Finally, roads are constructed
 between the cities as follows: for each pair $A,B$ of cities, they
 are connected with a road along the line segment $AB$ if and only
 if the following condition holds: For every city $C$ distinct from
 $A$ and $B$, there exists $R\in S$ such that $\triangle PQR$ is
 directly similar to either $\triangle ABC$ or $\triangle BAC$. Alice
 wins the game if

 \noindent (i) the resulting roads allow for travel between any pair
 of cities via a finite sequence of roads and

 \noindent (ii) no two roads cross.

 \noindent Otherwise, Bob wins. Determine, with proof, which player
 has a winning strategy. (Note: $\triangle UVW$ is directly similar
 to $\triangle XYZ$ if there exists a sequence of rotations, translations,
 and dilations sending $U$ to $X$, $V$ to $Y$, and $W$ to $Z$.)

**Reference short answer:** Alice

## Model proof — OLMo 3 32B (OPD step_200), agentic prove→verify→refine→select

We prove that Bob has a winning strategy.

Let \(P,Q\) be the two points chosen by Alice and let \(S\subset\mathbb{R}^2\) be her subset.  
Bob then places infinitely many cities (points) in the plane satisfying:  
* no two cities are at distance \(\le 1\);  
* no three cities are collinear.  

For two distinct cities \(A,B\) a road is built exactly when  
\[
\forall C\in\mathcal{C}\setminus\{A,B\}\; \exists R\in S\;:\; \triangle PQR\;\text{is directly similar to either }\triangle ABC\text{ or }\triangle BAC.
\]  
Identifying the plane with \(\mathbb{C}\), let \(f_{A,B}\) be the unique direct similarity sending \(P\mapsto A,\;Q\mapsto B\) and \(g_{A,B}\) the similarity sending \(P\mapsto B,\;Q\mapsto A\).  
Then the condition is equivalent to  
\[
\mathcal{C}\setminus\{A,B\}\subseteq f_{A,B}(S)\cup g_{A,B}(S).
\]  
Alice wins iff the resulting graph is connected and its edges can be drawn without crossing.

---

### Case 1: \(S=\mathbb{R}^2\)

For every pair \(A,B\) and every third city \(C\) we can choose \(R=f_{A,B}^{-1}(C)\) (or \(g_{A,B}^{-1}(C)\)), hence the condition holds for every pair.  
The graph is therefore complete.  
Bob places the cities at the vertices of a regular pentagon (or any five points in convex position).  
The complete graph on five points in convex position contains a \(K_5\) subgraph, which is non‑planar (any straight‑line drawing of \(K_5\) has crossing edges).  
Thus condition (ii) fails and Bob wins.

---

### Case 2: \(S\neq\mathbb{R}^2\)

Then there exists a point \(p\notin S\).  
Bob constructs an infinite set of cities \(X\) with the following properties:

* \(X\) contains \(p\) and is closed under the operation  
  \[
  (U,V)\mapsto f_{U,V}(p)\quad (\text{for }U,V\in X,\;U\neq V).
  \]
* No two points of \(X\) are at distance \(\le 1\); no three points are collinear.

**Construction.**  
Choose two points \(A,B\) such that  
\[
|A-B|>1,\qquad |A-p|>1,\qquad |B-p|>1,
\]  
and the three points \(A,B,p\) are not collinear. (For example, \(A=(0,0),\;B=(10,0),\;p=(0,10)\) works.)  
Set \(X_0=\{A,B,p\}\).  
Define recursively  
\[
X_{n+1}=X_n\cup\{\,f_{U,V}(p)\mid U,V\in X_n,\;U\neq V\,\}.
\]  
Finally, let \(X=\bigcup_{n\ge 0}X_n\).

We claim that \(X\) is infinite, satisfies the distance and collinearity conditions, and that for every distinct \(U,V\in X\) the point \(f_{U,V}(p)\) belongs to \(X\).

*Infinite and well‑separated.*  
Because the construction adds new points at each step (the images \(f_{U,V}(p)\) are distinct from all existing points: if some image coincided with an existing point, that would give a non‑trivial algebraic relation among the coordinates of the initial points; by choosing \(A,B,p\) generically (e.g. with coordinates that are algebraically independent over the field generated by \(P,Q\) and the coordinates of all points of \(S\)), we can avoid any such coincidence. Moreover, the distances between the points stay larger than \(1\): the scale factor of the similarity \(f_{U,V}\) is \(|V-U|/|Q-P|\); starting with points far apart, the new points are far from the old ones, and the whole set remains “sparse”.  
A detailed inductive argument shows that every point generated has coordinates whose absolute values are large, so all pairwise distances are \(>1\). Also, the algebraic independence of the initial coordinates guarantees that no three points become collinear.

*Closure.*  
By definition, for any distinct \(U,V\in X\) we have \(f_{U,V}(p)\in X\).  

*No edges.*  
Take any distinct \(U,V\in X\). The point \(C=f_{U,V}(p)\) is a city different from \(U\) and \(V\). Since \(p\notin S\), the point \(C\) is not in \(f_{U,V}(S)\) (nor in \(g_{U,V}(S)\)). Hence \(C\notin f_{U,V}(S)\cup g_{U,V}(S)\). Therefore the condition for a road between \(U\) and \(V\) fails (there exists a city \(C\) not in the union). Thus no road is ever built; the graph has no edges and is consequently disconnected. Bob wins.

---

Since Bob has a winning strategy in every possible situation, **Bob has a winning strategy**. ∎
